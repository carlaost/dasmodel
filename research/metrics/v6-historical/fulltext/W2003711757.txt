Predicting Cognitive Decline in Alzheimer Disease
Role of Platelet Amyloid Precursor Protein
Barbara Borroni, MD,* Francesca Colciaghi, PhD,† Silvana Archetti, PhD,‡ Elena Marcello, PhD,†
Luigi Caimi, MD,§ Monica Di Luca, PhD,† and Alessandro Padovani, MD, PhD*

Abstract: An altered pattern of platelet amyloid precursor protein
(APP) forms, consisting of a reduced ratio of the upper (130 kDa) to
the lower (110 to 106 kDa) immunoreactivity band (APPr), is associated with Alzheimer disease (AD), although in the early stages of AD
this pattern shows high variability. To explore whether APPr values at
baseline may predict the rate of cognitive decline, we evaluated patients with mild AD before and after 1 year of treatment with acetylcholinesterase inhibitors. Lower APPr at baseline was the only predictor of progressive cognitive decline, suggesting that this biomarker
might be a useful indicator of prognosis for patients with AD.
Key Words: amyloid precursor protein, acetylcholinesterase inhibitors, rate of cognitive decline
(Alzheimer Dis Assoc Disord 2004;18:32–34)

B

ecause Alzheimer disease (AD) has a mean duration of 6 to
10 years from diagnosis and is characterized by progressive cognitive impairment with great intraindividual and interindividual variability in rates of decline, one goal of research is
to identify a biologic marker that would assist in predicting the
rate of decline. At present, physicians can only give approximate answers, establishing the rate of decline by monitoring
patients during the course of the disease. A specific biologic
indicator of the rate of cognitive decline would help physicians
and caregivers determine how long patients would be able attend to the activities of daily living before they require institutionalization. One predictor of decline in AD may be an alteration in protein metabolism.
Amyloid precursor protein (APP) metabolism has been
found to be specifically altered in the platelets of patients with
sporadic AD.1,2 Although an altered pattern of platelet APP

METHODS
The study was carried out in a group of subjects with
probable AD consecutively evaluated at the Clinica Neurologica, University of Brescia, Italy. The study was conducted
in accordance with local clinical research regulations, and informed consent was obtained from all subjects and caregivers
when indicated. The diagnosis of probable AD was based on
criteria from the National Institute of Neurologic and Communicative Disorders and Stroke and Alzheimer’s Disease and
Related Disorders Association. Dementia severity was measured by means of the Clinical Dementia Rating scale and the
Mini-Mental State Examination (MMSE).5,6
The exclusion criteria used to ensure that participants
had probable AD as the cause of their dementia are reported
elsewhere.3 Further, only patients with AD and a Clinical Dementia Rating score ⱕ1 were enrolled.

Study Design
We conducted a longitudinal, open study. Patients were
evaluated at baseline and after 1 year of treatment with ChEIs
(donepezil 10 mg/day). In particular, at baseline all patients
underwent a clinical and neuropsychologic evaluation, genotyping of apolipoprotein E (ApoE), and venipuncture for platelet sample collection. According to the difference between the
MMSE scores at baseline (T0) and after 1 year (T1) (⌬MMSE
T1–T0), patients were classified as “no decliners” (⌬MMSE ⱖ
0) and “decliners” (⌬MMSE < 0).

RESULTS
Forty-eight of 55 patients with AD from the Center for
Ageing Brain and Neurodegenerative Diseases entered and
completed the study and were reevaluated at follow-up visits.
Seven patients dropped out because of adverse effects.
At baseline, patients with AD (mean ± SD, age = 70.7 ±
9.1 years, MMSE T0 = 21.2 ± 4.8) showed mean APPr values
of 0.40 ± 0.28. No association was found between ApoE genotype and APPr values at baseline (p = 0.30).
After 1 year, a significant cognitive decline was observed (MMSE T1 = 19.5 ± 5.6, MMSE T0 vs. MMSE T1, p <
0.001). According to ⌬MMSE (T1–T0), there were 20 “no decliners” and 28 “decliners,” whose demographic and clinical
characteristics are shown in Table 1.
The difference in MMSE scores was found to be significantly associated with APPr at baseline (p < 0.01), and there
was no association with demographic and clinical variables or
with ApoE genotype.

DISCUSSION
The main finding of this study is that cognitive decline at
1-year follow-up inversely correlates with baseline platelet
APPr in patients with AD treated with ChEIs. Indeed, patients
with lower platelet APPr at baseline showed higher rates of
decline despite ChEI therapy.
Few data exist to explain different rates of progression in
sporadic AD. Several authors have focused their attention on
vascular risk factors, claiming that hypertension, hypercholesterolemia, and diabetes might play a role in disease progression.4 In genetic studies, the finding of less responsiveness to
long-term ChEI therapy in subjects carrying ApoE ␧4 has not
been confirmed.7,8 Most previous reports have failed to identify a clear-cut predictor, either clinical or biologic, for rate of
decline and, similarly, for responsiveness to ChEIs.
Our study follows previous reports about the significance of platelet APPr in clinical practice. As previously
shown in a large sample of patients, platelet APPr is specifically associated with AD and is correlated with disease severity.3 Nevertheless, it was observed, especially in the early
stages of AD, that there was a high variability in APPr values.2
In this study, we confirmed that platelet APPr values are very
scattered in mild AD and that a relatively large proportion of
patients display very low APPr values. This finding is in agreement with autopsy studies showing that patients with AD and
very similar clinical impairment might present very different
degrees of neuropathologic changes.9 In this regard, the present study argues that in vivo a different rate of decline is determined by a different spectrum of amyloid burden. Indeed, it
has been previously reported that APP abnormalities in platelets of patients with AD parallel a dysfunction of APP processing in the central nervous system,10,11 thus suggesting that patients with lower APPr have a more severe and widespread
amyloid processing impairment.
We acknowledge that this study has some limitations,
and we admittedly understand that caution is needed before
considering platelet APPr as a true prognostic indicator of cognitive decline over time.
First, at a more general and theoretical level, neuropathologic verification and demonstration of the relationship

of platelet APPr with the peripheral compartment are still lacking. Moreover, the inclusion of a comparison group of untreated patients would have been helpful to fully answer the
question of whether APPr predicts rate of progression or drug
responsiveness, although it has been previously reported that
the symptomatic efficacy of donepezil is independent of APPr
values. Finally, the study was based on baseline APPr values,
and change in this value at follow-up would have added further
support to the association between biologic and clinical variables.
Despite these limitations, our data indicate the usefulness of platelets as a peripheral delegate for study of central
nervous system-related mechanisms beyond the diagnostic validity and further support the role of APP metabolism in the
pathogenesis of disease.
These findings shed some light on different rates of cognitive decline, suggesting that platelet APPr might be a useful
tool for prognostic evaluation of patients with AD.
