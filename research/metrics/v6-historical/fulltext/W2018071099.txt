Serum lipoprotein pattern variations in
dementia and ischemic stroke
Giubilei F, DAntona R, Antonini R, Lenzi GL, Ricci G, Fieschi C. Serum
lipoprotein pattern variations in dementia and ischemic stroke.
Acta Neurol Scand 1990: 81: 84-86.
Blood levels of triglycerides, total cholesterol, isolated lipoprotein fractions
(VLDL- LDL- and HDL-cholesterol) and apoproteins (Apo-A1 and Apo-B)
were examined in multi-infarct dementia, senile dementia of the Alzheimer
type, ischemic stroke associated with carotid atherosclerosis and in control
subjects. Forty patients divided into 10 consecutive patients for each
group were studied. Alzheimer patients showed mean total cholesterol
and Apo-B values significantly higher than control subjects. Apo-B was
significantly higher in stroke patients than in controls. The mean lowest
HDL-cholesterol (HDL-c) value was observed in stroke patients. No
significant differences in mean HDL-c levels were found between patients
with multi-infarct and Alzheimer dementia.

Reduced levels of high density lipoprotein cholesterol (HDL-c) are associated with atherosclerosis and
therefore with a risk factor for stroke (1-4) and
multi-infarct dementia (MID) (5). Patients with
senile dementia of the Alzheimer type (SDAT) have
been reported to have high HDL-c levels (5). This
difference has been proposed a a useful tool for
differential diagnosis (5).
We examined blood levels of triglycerides (TG),
total cholesterol (CHO), isolated lipoprotein
fractions (VLDL- LDL- and HDL-cholesterol), and
apoproteins (Apo-A1 and Apo-B) in 4 groups of
subjects: MID; SDAT; ischemic stroke associated
with carotid atherosclerosis; and controls.
Material and methods

Forty patients divided into 10 consecutive patients
for each group were studied. Patients presenting with
alcoholism, severe obesity, diabetes, liver and renal
diseases, and hypothyroidism were excluded.
The decline of mental functions was assessed by
administering the second part of the partially modified Blessed Dementia Scale (total score between 0
and 34) (6). Clinical history, neurological examination, Hachinski score, laboratory tests and CT
scan were used to differentiate SDAT from MID, as
well as to exclude other types of dementia.
Diagnosis of ischemic stroke was based on
neurological examination and CT scan findings. The
presence of carotid atherosclerosis was investigated
by non-invasive techniques (Doppler sonography or
echotomography) and/or cerebral angiography.
All control subjects had normal cerebral CT scan,

The patients were not following any dietary prescriptions, and did not take drugs associated with
alteration of HDL levels. Two of 10 patients with
stroke and 1 of 10 patients with MID had severe
hemiparesis with functional impairment in daily activities. However, all patients were able to sit.
Blood samples were collected into test-tubes and
blindly processed by operators unaware of the clinical diagnosis. CHO and TG were determined in
whole plasma and isolated lipoprotein fractions (7)
by enzymatic colorimetric methods (8,9).
HDL-c levels were determined after precipitation
of apoprotein-B containing lipoprotein (10). Apoproteins (Apo-A1 and Apo-B) were essayed by
radial immunodiffusion.
Dunnett’s test was used for the statistical evaluation of the results.

Discussion

Munckle et a1 ( 5 ) described HDL-c levels significantly lower in 5 MID patients compared with 12
SDAT patients, while Lehtonen et a1 (1 1) found no
difference in 68 patients, with SDAT (n = 22), MID
(n = 23) or without dementia (n = 23). Our data
confirm the latter observation of an overlap in HDL
values between MID and SDAT patient with no
significantdifference in the mean HDL-c levels. This
lack of significance may be caused by the relatively
small sample size. However, MID is not necessarily
related to atherosclerosis. Cardiac embolism
(12-14) or small vessel diseases secondary to hypertension (15-17) are frequently associated with the
pathogenesis of MID. For example, 3 of 10 MID
patients had a cardiac pathogenesis and no demonstrable atherosclerotic lesions.
Our results in the stroke group indirectly confirm
the importance of HDL-c levels as a risk factor in
vascular disease (18-21), since this group had the
lowest mean HDL value.
The high levels of CHO and Apo-B observed in
our SDAT patients remain unexplained at this time.
CHO was also significantly elevated in our stroke
group, and previous data show that high CHO is
associated with a high incidence of intra- and extracranial atherosclerotic lesions in males, as documented by cerebral angiography (22).
We could not confirm that the HDL-c value can
be used to discriminate between MID and SDAT.
