Abstract
Background: Cleavage of the amyloid precursor protein
(APP) by -site APP-cleaving enzyme and -secretase results
in the generation of amyloid- (A) peptides that aggregate
and deposit as senile plaques in brains of Alzheimer disease
patients. Due to the fundamental role -secretase plays in
the proteolysis of a number of proteins including Notch,
pharmacological inhibition of -secretase has been associated with mechanism-based toxicities. Therefore, efforts
have focussed on the modulation of -secretase activity to

deleterious activity on Notch processing. Objective: Here,
we describe the in vitro and in vivo characterisation of a novel -secretase modulator, GSM-10h, and investigate the potential for shorter A peptides to induce neurotoxicity in rat
primary cortical neurons. Methods: The effect of GSM-10h
on A levels was investigated in SH-SY5Y cells expressing

Materials and Methods
Compound Synthesis
The synthesis of GSM-10h has been described elsewhere [14].
Cell Treatments
Human neuroblastoma SH-SY5Y cells expressing APP695
Swedish variantK595N/M596L (SH-SY5Y-APPswe) were cultured in
Dulbecco’s modified Eagle’s medium/Ham’s F12 (1: 1) (Invitrogen, Paisley, UK) containing 10% fetal bovine serum, 2 m M glutamine and 400 g/ml geneticin (Invitrogen) at 37 ° C with 5%
CO2/95% air. For 24-hour treatment with inhibitor, cells were cultured to confluency in 96-well plates in medium lacking geneticin. Compounds were dissolved in dimethylsulphoxide (DMSO)
(Sigma-Aldrich, Poole, UK) and added to cells at the appropriate
test concentration, ensuring that the final DMSO concentration
was !0.5%. A DMSO solvent control was included in each experiment. The effect of compounds on cell viability was assessed using the WST-1 cell proliferation assay (Roche Diagnostics, Burgess Hill, UK).
In vivo TASTPM Studies
TASTPM mice that express human APPsweK595N/M596L and
presenilin-1 (PS-1)M146V transgenes in the brain [15] and were
aged approximately 60 days were used for in vivo efficacy studies.
In the time-course study, TASTPM mice (mixed gender; n = 11/
group) were orally administered vehicle (1% methylcellulose in
water) or GSM-10h (100 mg/kg) at a volume of 10 ml/kg. At 3, 6,
15 or 24 h after drug administration, the mice were euthanised
in accordance with the 1986 Animals (Scientific Procedures) Act
by intraperitoneal injection of a lethal dose of pentobarbitone
sodium (Euthatal; Rhone Merieux, Harlow, UK) and blood and
brain samples taken. For the dose-response studies, male
TASTPM mice (n = 9–10/group) were orally administered either
vehicle (1% methylcellulose in water) or GSM-10h (3, 10, 30 or 100
mg/kg) at a volume of 10 ml/kg. In the acute study, mice were
euthanised 6 h after receiving a single dose of vehicle or GSM10h. In the subchronic study, mice were given GSM-10h or vehicle once daily for 5 days. Six hours after the final dose, the mice
were euthanised as described above. In all studies, brains were
removed and hemisected. The left brain hemisection was snap
frozen for subsequent measurement of A peptides. In the doseresponse studies, a 10-mm section of ileum was taken, 30–40 mm
from the junction with the caecum. The contents were gently removed and the tissues snap frozen for subsequent TaqMan쏐 analysis. From mice in the subchronic study, whole thymi were dissected, rinsed with cold Roswell Park Memorial Institute (RPMI)
medium (Invitrogen) and placed in RPMI medium for subsequent flow cytometry.

The main protein components of senile plaques are amyloid- (A) peptides, predominantly 40 or 42 amino acids in length (A40, A42), which are generated following
sequential cleavage of the amyloid precursor protein
(APP) by -site APP-cleaving enzyme 1 (BACE1) and secretase [3]. Due to its pivotal role in A generation, secretase is an attractive target for therapeutic intervention. A number of potent -secretase inhibitors have been
identified from cell-based screens and shown to inhibit
A40 and A42 production in cells and in APP transgenic mice [4]. However, due to the critical role -secretase
plays in the intramembrane proteolysis of a number of
other proteins, these compounds produced mechanismbased toxicities in preclinical disease-relevant models [4–
6]. These adverse events occurred in the gastrointestinal
tract and immune system and were primarily due to inhibition of Notch cleavage and subsequent downregulation of the Notch signalling pathway.
Modulation of -secretase to inhibit selectively A42
production with no effect on the cleavage of Notch and
other -secretase substrates is thus desirable to avoid the
toxicological issues hindering the development of secretase inhibitors. Modulation of -secretase was initially demonstrated by non-steroidal anti-inflammatory
drugs (NSAIDs) [7, 8]. NSAIDs such as indomethacin
and sulindac sulphide were found to decrease A42 production selectively by shifting cleavage away from the
amyloidogenic A42 site towards cleavage at the A37 and
A38 sites. Encouragingly, no effect on Notch cleavage
was observed. Subsequent drug discovery efforts resulted
in the identification of more potent -secretase modulators [9–13].
We recently reported on the generation of a potent and
selective -secretase modulator GSM-10h: {(2S,4R)-1{(1 R)-4-methyl-1-[6-(trif luoromethyl)-3-pyridinyl]
pentyl}-2-[4-(trifluoromethyl)phenyl]-4-piperidinyl}acetic acid [14]. This compound inhibited A42 production
in cells with no effect on Notch cleavage. Pharmacokinetic profiling of this compound in vivo demonstrated
low clearance and excellent oral bioavailability. Despite
containing a carboxylic acid and being zwitterionic,
GSM-10h showed good central nervous system (CNS)
penetration in the mouse with a brain-blood concentration ratio of 0.74:1 [14]. These findings suggested GSM10h had the potential to lower brain A42 levels in vivo.
We now describe the in vitro and in vivo pharmacology
of this compound and investigate the potential neurotoxic properties of shorter A peptides.

Determination of Drug Concentrations
Blood (n = 10–11) and brain (n = 2) samples were analysed for
GSM-10h using a method based on protein precipitation and
liquid chromatography-mass spectrometry (LC-MS)/MS analysis. Prior to analysis, brain samples were homogenised with
an equal weight of deionised water. To samples of blood (25 l
diluted with 25 l of water) and brain homogenate (50 l),
acetonitrile/ammonium acetate (10 m M; 8: 2; 125 l) containing
an appropriate internal standard was added. The samples were
mixed thoroughly (mechanical shaking for 20 min), and then
centrifuged (2,465 g for 15 min at room temperature). An aliquot
of the resulting supernatant was analysed for GSM-10h by reverse-phase LC-MS/MS using a heat-assisted electrospray interface in negative ion mode (Sciex API 3000) and a Synergi FusionRP column (50 ! 2 mm i.d.; Thermo Fischer). The mobile phase
was delivered as a linear gradient of 20–95% acetonitrile/ammonium acetate (10 m M containing 0.1% v/v formic acid) over 0.8
min. The final composition was held for 0.8 min before returning
to the initial composition. The nominal multiple reaction monitoring transition for the free base of GSM-10h was 515–210. Concentration ranges for the assays were 0.02–30 g/ml for the blood
samples with a lower limit of quantification of 0.02 g/ml, and
0.01–60 g/g for the brain samples with a lower limit of quantification of 0.01 g/g.
