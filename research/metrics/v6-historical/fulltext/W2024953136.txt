Abstract
In this study, we demonstrated for the first time an increased
CD44 gene expression in lymphocytes derived from Alzheimer’s disease (AD) patients in comparison with healthy
subjects. CD44 is a surface antigen expressed by cells of the
immune and central nervous system as well as in a variety of
other tissues. Functioning as adhesion molecule, CD44 is furthermore involved in driving immune response into infected
tissues, including the CNS. We also found that lymphocytes
of the same patients expressed significant levels of unfolded
p53 isoform, confirming what we already demonstrated in
fibroblasts and lymphocytes derived from other cohorts of
AD patients. A correlation between p53 and CD44 expression has been well demonstrated in cancer cells, suggesting
that CD44 could be a target gene of mutant p53, or either
mutant p53 could lack its ability to negatively regulate CD44
expression. The contemporaneous increased expression of
unfolded p53 and CD44 in AD lymphocytes may suggest

Introduction

prediction of the incipient disease allows an earlier pharmacological approach. In this context, identification of
new putative biomarkers [1] can increase the accuracy of
clinical diagnosis but, more importantly, it makes earlier
diagnosis possible. In the last 20 years, independent scientific groups have looked for biological and biochemical alterations in peripheral tissues of AD patients, because of their easy accessibility. The concept of peripheral biomarker implies the involvement of many cell
phenotypes in the disease and suggests that AD can be
considered as a systemic disease. The major difficulty in
the universal acceptance of a peripheral biomarker for

Methods
Subjects
Sporadic AD patients and healthy subjects were enrolled at the
Department of Neurology, MSWiA Hospital, Warsaw, Poland.
The demographic and clinical information is reported in table 1.
Control subjects were individuals with no clinical signs of neurological or psychiatric diseases. All these subjects were examined
by a senior neurologist or geriatrician, and diagnosis of dementia
was made according to DSM-IV and the NINCDS-ADRDA criteria. Dementia was diagnosed based upon interview, objective and
neurological examination, cognitive evaluation, and laboratory
and radiological (CT scan) investigation. Cognitive status was
quantified using the Mini-Mental State Examination.
Quantitative Real-Time PCR
Real-time PCR was performed according to Ferrari-Toninelli
et al. [24]. The oligonucleotide sequences of the primers used are
as follows for CD44: forward primer 5ⴕ-TTC ATC CTC GGG TGT
GCT AT-3ⴕ, reverse primer 5ⴕ-GTA CAC CCC AAC CTC AGT
GG-3ⴕ. The PCR program was initiated by 10 min at 95 ° C before
40 cycles, each for 15 s at 95 ° C and 1 min at 60 ° C.
Immunoprecipitation Analysis
p53 conformational state was analyzed by immunoprecipitation as previously detailed [2]. Briefly, 100 ␮g of protein extracts
was used for immunoprecipitation experiments performed with
1 ␮g of the conformation-specific antibodies PAb1620 (wild-type
specific) or PAb240 (mutant specific).
Statistical Evaluation
Results are given as mean 8 standard error mean values. Statistical significance of differences was determined by t test. Significance was accepted at p ! 0.05.

Crystallographic studies on p53 tertiary structure have
been often correlated with its gene mutations. Structural
studies have allowed classification of p53 mutant protein
into two large categories: (a) mutations which interfere
with DNA sequence binding, and (b) mutations that determine an important conformational alteration. Interestingly, exposure of wild-type p53 to metal chelators
such as EDTA, oxidant compounds [4–6], or synthetic
nitric oxide donors (such as S nitro-N-acetylpenicilamine
and S-nitrosoglutathione) [7] resulted in a rapid switch
from wild-type to the unfolded ‘mutant-like’ form, suggesting that conformational changes can be also the result of posttranscriptional modifications. In addition,
we demonstrated that the exposure to nanomolar concentrations of ␤-amyloid peptides 1–40 induced the expression of a mutant-like p53 isoform in different cellular models [8, 9]. It is noteworthy that mutant p53 was
found to acquire new transcriptional functions [10–14].
Whether unfolded p53 found in AD is endowed with
such functional activity is not known. Anyway, learning
from the cancer field, some genes, which correlated with
mutant p53, may have relevance in the pathogenesis of
AD. Genes such as IGF1R, IL6 and CD44 [15–17] attracted our attention because of their observed role in the immune system. In fact, in recent years the relevance of
immune response in AD has been revised [18–20], suggesting a surveillance role of the immune system before
disease onset, rather than a harmful event. Schwartz’s
group has obtained a large body of evidence suggesting
that under normal conditions CNS immune surveillance, primarily mediated by T lymphocytes recognizing
CNS antigens, is important for maintaining cognitive
abilities and the capacity for neuronal cell renewal in
adulthood [21–23]. In this regard, we took into consideration the CD44 gene for its role in driving immune response in infected tissues, including the CNS. The aim

Results and Discussion

p53 conformational state in lymphocytes derived from
AD and non-AD subjects was studied by the immunoprecipitation technique using two conformational-specific
antibodies, PAb1620 and PAb240, which discriminate
folded versus unfolded p53 tertiary structure [8]. In particular, the PAb1620 epitope is exposed in the wild-type
p53 form that is competent for DNA binding and depends
on the tertiary folding of the protein. In contrast, the
PAb240 epitope is cryptic in the protein that is competent
for DNA binding but is exposed by denaturation. Immunoprecipitates were then analyzed by Western blot analysis with a rabbit polyclonal p53 antibody. Data were expressed as the ratio of PAb240/PAb1620. A statistically
significant unfolded p53 increase was found in AD patients in comparison to control subjects (fig. 1a). These
data confirmed what we already found in another cohort
of patients [3].
The new result of the present study is the expression
of CD44 mRNA in AD patients (fig. 1b). CD44 has been
extensively studied in the lymphoid compartment as a
molecule involved in the development and differentiation of T and B cells and an activation marker increased
on cell surface following stimulation by antigen and mitogen [25]. Furthermore, CD44 exerts an important role
in addressing lymphocytes in infected tissues, including
the CNS [26]. Increased expression of CD44 variant 6 on
monocyte surface has been found to correlate with the

CD44 is also studied as a tumor marker. Numerous
studies correlated increased CD44 expression with tumor growth and metastatic potential of tumor cells [31–
33]. In addition, in many tumor tissue samples the expression of CD44 correlates with high expression of p53.
Godar et al. [34] demonstrated that high levels of CD44
in breast cancer samples correlated with mutant p53, and
they concluded that wild-type p53 negatively regulates
CD44 expression. On the other hand, another study demonstrated that mutant p53 in lymphoblastoid cells induced the activation of CD44 gene, indicating CD44 as a

target gene of mutant p53 [17]. Whether or not CD44 is a
target gene of unfolded p53 is now matter of investigation. The correlation between CD44 and unfolded p53 in
AD can assume an intriguing significance also in the
possible role of the immune system in AD pathology. Recent evidence from animal models suggests that the activated microglia that surround senile plaques have been
recruited from the blood [35]. Our working hypothesis is
that cross-talk between unfolded p53 and CD44 in AD
lymphocytes may have relevance in mediating immune
response in AD pathology.
