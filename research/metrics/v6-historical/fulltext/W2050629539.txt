Abstract
Background: Alzheimer’s disease (AD) is the leading cause
of dementia. Currently, no definitive diagnostic test for AD
exists. Cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) concentrations of amyloid ␤
(A␤1-42) peptides and total tau proteins (T-tau) may serve as
biomarkers for AD. Aim: The objective of this study was to
investigate the usefulness of CSF A␤1-42 and T-tau analyses
in the diagnosis of AD with Tunisians. Methods: We focused
on three groups originating from Central Tunisian that
matched in age (range 48–85): healthy controls (n = 53), AD
patients (n = 93) and non-Alzheimer (nAD) dementia (n = 35)
patients. A␤1-42 and T-tau levels were measured in CSF by
sandwich enzyme-linked immunosorbent assay. Results:
The ratio of T-tau/A␤1-42 at baseline yielded a sensitivity of
85.3% for detection of AD and the specificity was 84.8% to
differentiate controls and nAD dementia. Conclusion: Our
findings confirm the use of T-tau/A␤1-42 ratio in the discrimination of AD patients from all other patients.

Introduction

Alzheimer’s disease (AD) is the most common cause
of dementia in the elderly [1] affecting more than 5% of
all people aged 665 and about 25% of those aged 685 [2].
In 2004, the Tunisian Study of Health and Aging estimated that the prevalence of AD is 3.7%. Nearly 25,000
Tunisians met criteria for AD. This number is increasing
every year because of the constant growth of the older
population. It is estimated that the number of AD patients will reach 46,000 in Tunisia by 2014 [3]. Prevalence
studies of AD have found higher rates of AD among elderly people with fewer years of education [4, 5].
Without truly effective therapies, AD will soon become a public health crisis [6]. Meanwhile, the advent of
new therapeutic avenues calls for an improved early and
differential diagnosis of AD [7]. So far, the diagnosis of
AD as outlined in the NINCDS-ADRDA criteria is based
on clinical neuropsychological examinations, identification of typical symptoms of AD and exclusion of other
known causes of dementia [2, 7]. The accuracy of the clinical diagnosis at the primary care level and in general
hospitals is probably ever lower, especially in the early
stages of the disease when the symptoms are vague and

Materials and Methods
Patients
All assessments were carried out after obtaining written informing consent from patients and receiving approval from the
local ethics committee. A total sample of 181 subjects, all from
Central Tunisia, presented themselves to Sahloul University Hospital, between January 2005 and March 2008. All participants underwent a complete clinical investigation, including medical history, neurological and neuropsychological examination, MiniMental State Examination (MMSE), screening laboratory tests
and neuroimaging consisting of magnetic resonance imaging
(MRI). MRI has shown a great reduction in medial temporal lobe
and hippocampal volume of patients with AD as compared to
controls and to nAD dementias.
The demographic data of patients are presented in table 1. The
following groups of patients were investigated. The group of probable AD patients (n = 93) consists of patients diagnosed according
to the criteria of the National Institute of Neurological and Communicative Disorders and Stoke-Alzheimer’s Disease and Related
Disorders Association (NINCDS-ADRDA) [7]. These patients
were further classified, according their MMSE scores, as follows:
mild AD (MMSE 624), moderate AD (MMSE 11–23) and severe
AD (MMSE ^10).

The patients with nAD dementia (n = 35) were diagnosed according to the criteria of the Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of
Mental Disorders, 4th edition (DSM-IV) [15]. The following subjects were included in this group: vascular dementia (n = 18),
mixed dementia (n = 7), frontotemporal dementia (n = 5), Lewy
body dementia (n = 3) and unclassified dementia (n = 2). The control group consisted of 53 age-matched subjects. The controls
were recruited at the center, the Department of Neurology, Sahloul
Hospital in Tunisia. Inclusion criteria were: absence of memory
complaints and some other cognitive symptoms; preservation of
general cognitive functioning, and no active neurological or psychiatric disease. Individuals with other medical conditions that
did not affect cognition were not excluded.
Laboratory Procedures
CSF was obtained from 38 controls, 73 AD patients and 35
nAD patients. The CSF was stored in polypropylene tubes at
–80 ° C, and analyzed after the clinical follow-up of the study was
completed. For the 36 patients who did not undergo a successful
lumbar puncture at baseline, the procedure did not deliver usable
CSF.
CSF T-tau and A␤1-42 Determination
CSF T-tau and A␤1-42 levels were determined using a commercially available sandwich enzyme-linked immunosorbent assay (ELISA, Innotest hTAU-Ag, Innotest ␤-amyloid 1-42; Belgium). Briefly, to measure A␤1-42 levels in CSF, 25 ␮l of undiluted CSF from patients or standards included in the kit were
applied to microtiter plates precoated with antibody. A second
conjugate was applied to incubate for 30 min. The wells were then
washed and substrate solution was applied. The reaction was
stopped with sulfuric acid, and the color reaction was read at 450
nm. To measure CSF T-tau, undiluted CSF or standards were applied to each well of the microtiter plate simultaneously with conjugate. After an overnight incubation at room temperature followed by washing steps, two biotinylated tau-specific monoclonal
antibodies were applied to incubate for 30 min. The wells were
then washed and substrate solution was applied. The reaction was
stopped with sulfuric acid, and the color reaction was read at
450 nm.

brain tissue pathology or brain biopsy [9, 10]. In view of
this, the need for specific AD markers is great [11]. Biological markers are essential to help in the development
of disease-modifying therapies and to identify individuals at high risk or in the earliest clinical stages. Since cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) is in direct contact with the extracellular space of the central nervous system, biochemical changes taking place in the brain should be reliably
reflected. Three AD biomarkers extensively reported in
the literature are A␤1-42, total tau protein (T-tau) and
phospho-tau protein, which are the major components of
brain senile plaque and neurofibrillary tangles respectively [10].
Many studies have shown that CSF T-tau is increased
and A␤1-42 is decreased in patients with dementia from
AD relative to age-matched controls [7, 9]. The combination of CSF A␤1-42 and T-tau has also been assessed, either as a ratio or by using more complex approaches with
improved specificity, sometimes at the expense of sensitivity, for dementia from AD [12–14]. In the present comparative study, we prospectively assessed CSF A␤1-42
and T-tau and concentrations in a large cohort of Tunisian patients with AD and other types of dementia. Our
intention was to confirm the usefulness of the CSF biomarkers for distinguishing AD patients from non-Alzheimer (nAD) dementias.

Statistical Analyses
All statistical analyses were performed by SPSS software for
Windows version 10.1. Comparisons of medians across groups
were separately assessed by non-parametric statistical design including the Kruskal-Wallis test. Pairwise comparisons between
groups then were performed with the Mann-Whitney U test. ANCOVA was used to analyze the covariance of age and education
in the variables under study. Correlations between different variables were calculated by Pearson’s correlation test or by Spearman’s correlation test. Diagnostic accuracy was assessed by using
receiver operating characteristic curves (ROC). Statistical significance was set at p ! 0.05.

Discussion

These studies complementing the molecular and neuropsychological analysis open an avenue for research on
potential biomarkers, especially with the purpose of optimizing early diagnostic tools for AD, and the groups at
risk of a neurodegenerative disease [16–18]. Therefore, research on biological markers in general is not only of interest for therapeutic approaches, but also for the design
of new AD drugs. Previous studies have used ELISA for
T-tau and A␤1-42 determinations in AD and normal
samples of CSF, and found a correlation with neuropathological observations [18, 19]. In this context, we
compared the power of low CSF A␤1-42, high T-tau levels
and the combination of these biomarkers to distinguish
patients with AD from subjects with other dementias and
controls. We have chosen the Tunisian population for genetic and geographic peculiarities that can represent a
powerful tool in the study of a genetic and multifactorial
disorder.
Several studies performed so far have reported a decreased concentration of A␤1-42 in CSF of patients with
AD, and our results are in agreement with these reports
[12–15, 20, 21], although this is not the case in all studies
