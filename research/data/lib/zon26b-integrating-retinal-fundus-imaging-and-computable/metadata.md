## Abstract

Alzheimer’s disease (AD) is characterized by substantial clinical and pathological heterogeneity, complicating diagnosis and treatment. To address this challenge, we propose a novel multimodal framework combining fundus imaging and computable phenotyping to identify distinct AD subphenotypes and their associations with early- and late-onset disease. By analyzing retinal vasculature and optic nerve structures, we extract interpretable biomarkers linked to AD progression, employing advanced statistical and machine learning techniques to uncover their relationships with disease subphenotypes. Our approach not only reveals key retinal signatures predictive of AD subphenotypes but also integrates these findings into a large language model (LLM)-based diagnostic system, enabling automated classification of early- and late-onset AD with explainable AI-driven insights. The LLM component further enhances clinical utility by generating personalized recommendations for patient management, bridging gaps between ophthalmological data and neurological applications. This study demonstrates the feasibility of using non-invasive retinal imaging and AI-powered subphenotyping to improve early detection and stratification of AD, offering a scalable and interpretable tool for precision medicine. Our findings highlight the potential of retinal biomarkers as proxies for neurodegenerative changes, while the LLM framework ensures clinical translatability by providing actionable diagnostic support. By unifying ophthalmology, neurology, and artificial intelligence, this work advances personalized AD diagnostics and lays the foundation for targeted therapeutic strategies.
