## Abstract

Imaging-based confirmation such as PET/CSF has long anchored Alzheimers disease (AD) diagnosis, yet cost, invasiveness, and limited access constrain scale. In 2025, regulated plasma assays, most notably p-tau217, have reached clinic-ready performance, reframing blood tests as a reliable Level-2 anchor, while passive digital biomarkers (DBMs; speech, wearables, smartphones) offer low-burden reach at Level-1. We synthesize a two-tier machine-learning triage that links DBM pre-screening to p-tau217-centered confirmation, and we formalize evaluation beyond AUC to a calibration net benefit resource framework with thresholds tied to pre-test probability. Across memory clinics and primary care, p-tau217 approaches PET/CSF (AUC 0.940.96); dual cutoffs yield clear rule-in/rule-out for about 8388% with only 1217% indeterminate and enable 5785% reductions in CSF/amyloid-PET, while sequential blood then tau-PET cuts trial-screening imaging by approximately 94%. Evidence across hundreds of DBM studies shows promising discrimination but sparse external validation and probability calibration, supporting DBMs primarily as pre-screeners that require domain-robust training and transparent reporting. Finally, we codify fairness-first deployment: subgroup calibration and threshold adjustment for device, language, age 80, and kidney dysfunction (eGFR<45), aligned with TRIPOD-AI/STARD-AI/PROBAST-AI/DECIDE-AI. In summary, an anchor-at-scale paradigm, digital-as-screen, blood-as-anchor, offers a practical, regulated, and economically defensible path from research prototypes to real-world AD services.
