## Abstract

Alzheimer's disease (AD) is a condition that severely affects cognitive and linguistic abilities. Spontaneous speech analysis via machine learning (ML) is a promising, non-invasive diagnostic pathway for this disease. Yet, most work centers around the English speaking population, restricting applicability globally. This review integrates the multilingual, cross-lingual, and non-English ML approaches for the identification of AD from spontaneous speech. We examine the leading non-English data sets, such as those in DementiaBank, and projects such as the MultiConAD initiative. We assess the ADReSS-M challenge's novel contribution to the current state of the art in cross-lingual research and contrast competing approaches, i.e., monolingual analysis, zero-shot learning, and transfer learning. Dominant trends, such as the emergence of large language models (LLMs) and language-agnostic features, are discussed alongside present traps such as data sparsity and heterogeneity. Future directions highlight the merit of developing richer multilingual resources, sophisticated transfer learning methods, and the identification of language-agnostic biomarkers to design equitable and efficient tools for diagnosis globally.
