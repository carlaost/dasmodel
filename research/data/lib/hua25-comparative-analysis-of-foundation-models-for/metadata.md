## Abstract

Electroencephalogram (EEG) analysis offers a noninvasive, cost-effective approach for Alzheimer’s disease (AD) detection, yet challenges persist due to EEG’s complexity and noise sensitivity. The emerging foundation model may provide a solution to mitigate such challenges. This study conducts a systematic comparison of foundation models for EEG-based AD classification, evaluating EEG-specific models (EEGPT, LaBraM, BIOT), general time series models (TimesFM, UniTS), and traditional deep learning baselines (PatchTST, TST, TCN) across six public datasets. Using standardized preprocessing, patient-level data splits, and comprehensive evaluation metrics (accuracy, F1-score, precision and recall), we demonstrate that EEG-specific models like BIOT achieve strong performance (e.g., 98.77% precision on BrainLat) but exhibit dataset-dependent variability, while general time series models show moderate transferability. Traditional architectures like TCN remain competitive, underscoring trade-offs between pre-training benefits and task-specific tuning. Our findings highlight the importance of domain-specific adaptations, emphasizing the need for standardized benchmarking to bridge research and practical deployment. This work provides actionable insights for model selection and advocates for scalable frameworks to advance EEG-based AD diagnosis.
