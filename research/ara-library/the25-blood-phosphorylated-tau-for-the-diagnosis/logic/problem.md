# Problem Specification

> Grounding: abstract-only. All content below is derived from the published abstract (metadata.md). Detail beyond the abstract is marked "Not available from provided input (no full text)".

## Observations

### O1: p-tau biomarkers are promising but unsynthesised
- **Statement**: Plasma phosphorylated tau (p-tau) biomarkers show promise to transform clinical management of Alzheimer's disease by providing more accessible and cost-effective diagnostic tools, and have emerged as leading contenders for clinical implementation.
- **Evidence**: Abstract, BACKGROUND (metadata.md).
- **Implication**: There is clinical demand for accessible AD diagnostics; blood p-tau is the frontrunner.

### O2: No comprehensive meta-analysis existed
- **Statement**: At the time of this study there had been no comprehensive meta-analyses of the diagnostic performance of plasma p-tau biomarkers.
- **Evidence**: Abstract, BACKGROUND (metadata.md).
- **Implication**: The comparative diagnostic accuracy of individual p-tau assays was not quantitatively established.

### O3: A large evidence base is available to synthesise
- **Statement**: A database search (Embase, MEDLINE, PubMed, Scopus, Web of Science; 1984–Dec 2024) identified 6429 studies; 312 were assessed for eligibility and 113 were included, comprising 29,625 unique individuals.
- **Evidence**: Abstract, FINDINGS (metadata.md).
- **Implication**: Enough primary diagnostic-accuracy studies exist to support pooled bivariate meta-analysis of multiple assays.

### O4: Widespread methodological weakness in the primary literature
- **Statement**: Approximately 90% of included studies were rated as high risk of bias for not having used predefined or externally derived thresholds.
- **Evidence**: Abstract, FINDINGS (metadata.md); QUADAS-2 assessment.
- **Implication**: Pooled accuracy estimates may be optimistic; reported thresholds were often data-derived within the same study.

## Gaps

### G1: Unknown comparative diagnostic accuracy of p-tau assays
- **Statement**: The pooled sensitivity, specificity, AUROC, and diagnostic odds ratio of individual plasma p-tau assays for biologically defined AD were unquantified and unranked.
- **Caused by**: O1, O2.
- **Existing attempts**: Numerous single-cohort primary diagnostic-accuracy studies (the 113 included studies) reported assay performance individually.
- **Why they fail**: Individual studies vary in reference standard, threshold derivation, and population; none provided a pooled, cross-assay comparison.

### G2: Uncertain certainty and bias of the underlying evidence
- **Statement**: The certainty of the evidence base and the extent of methodological bias across p-tau diagnostic studies were not systematically characterised.
- **Caused by**: O2, O4.
- **Existing attempts**: Individual studies did not uniformly apply QUADAS-2 or GRADE.
- **Why they fail**: Without a systematic review, threshold-related bias and evidence certainty could not be quantified across the field.

## Key Insight
- **Insight**: A PRISMA-DTA systematic review with bivariate random-effects meta-analysis, stratified by individual p-tau assay and benchmarked against biological reference standards (amyloid-PET, tau-PET, CSF, neuropathology), can rank assays and reveal that p-tau217 outperforms the others while exposing pervasive threshold-related risk of bias.
- **Derived from**: O1, O2, O3, O4.
- **Enables**: An evidence-graded ranking of p-tau assays for clinical implementation prioritisation.

## Assumptions
- A1: The included primary studies' reported diagnostic-accuracy data (sensitivity/specificity pairs) are extractable and comparable enough for bivariate pooling.
- A2: Amyloid-PET, tau-PET, CSF, and neuropathology are acceptable biological reference standards for "biologically defined" Alzheimer's disease.
