# Problem Specification

This is an annual public-health surveillance and health-economics report (Alzheimer's Association "Facts and Figures"). Its "problem" is descriptive: to quantify the current and projected public-health impact of Alzheimer's disease (AD) and other dementias in the United States — prevalence/incidence, mortality/morbidity, caregiving, the dementia care workforce, and the use and costs of care — and, in the 2026 Special Report, to characterize Americans' understanding of and engagement with lifelong brain health.

## Observations

### O1: A large and rapidly growing prevalent population
- **Statement**: An estimated 7.4 million Americans age 65+ live with clinical Alzheimer's dementia in 2026; 74% are 75+. Projected to reach 13.8 million by 2060. Prevalence rises steeply with age: 5.2% (65-74), 13.8% (75-84), 35.8% (85+).
- **Evidence**: Figures 4, 5, 8; Box 1; §3.2, §3.12.1; endnotes A2, A5 (CHAP + U.S. Census projections; Rajan et al.).
- **Implication**: Population aging (65+ growing from ~65M in 2026 to ~82M by 2050; 85+ share rising) will drive continued growth absent prevention/cure breakthroughs.

### O2: Mortality burden is large and rising while other causes fall
- **Statement**: 116,022 official AD deaths in 2024; AD was the 6th-leading cause of death overall and 5th among those 65+. Between 2000 and 2024, AD deaths rose 134.1% while heart-disease deaths fell 3.8% and HIV deaths fell 66.4%. The AD death rate peaked at 40.7/100,000 in 2020 (COVID-19) then fell to 34.1 in 2024.
- **Evidence**: Figures 9, 10; Tables 6, 7; §4.1-4.4 (CDC/NCHS).
- **Implication**: Death certificates undercount AD's true mortality contribution ("death with vs death from dementia").

### O3: Care depends heavily on unpaid family caregivers who bear health costs
- **Statement**: In 2025, 12.7 million unpaid caregivers provided 19.6 billion hours of care valued at $446.3 billion. 59% of dementia caregivers rate emotional stress as high/very high; depression prevalence 30-40%.
- **Evidence**: Figures 11, 12, 13; Tables 8-13; §5 (BRFSS, NAC/AARP, Alzheimer's Association poll).
- **Implication**: Dementia caregiving is more intensive and health-damaging than non-dementia caregiving.

### O4: A strained and insufficient dementia care workforce
- **Statement**: Only ~7,066-7,454 certified geriatricians in 2021 (source figures differ) versus ~23,953 needed in 2021 and 32,521 projected needed by 2050; direct-care worker turnover ~79% (home care) to ~99% (nursing assistants); median direct-care wage $17.36/hr (2024).
- **Evidence**: Tables 14, 15; §6 (American Geriatrics Society, Bureau of Labor Statistics).
- **Implication**: Diagnosis and treatment capacity (including for new biomarker testing and anti-amyloid therapies) is a bottleneck.

### O5: Enormous and rising costs concentrated in Medicare/Medicaid
- **Statement**: Total 2026 payments for people 65+ with AD/other dementias estimated at $409 billion (Medicare+Medicaid = $263B, 64%; out-of-pocket $103B, 25%). Per-person Medicare payments ~3x higher ($46,141 vs $15,499); Medicaid ~22x ($7,158 vs $322). Projected to approach $1 trillion by 2050.
- **Evidence**: Figure 15; Tables 16, 18-24; §7 (Lewin Model, MCBS 2018, Medicare 100% sample 2019, CMS).
- **Implication**: Fiscal pressure on public programs (Medicare Hospital Insurance Trust Fund projected into deficit).

### O6: Substantial underdiagnosis and demographic disparities
- **Statement**: Only ~half of Medicare beneficiaries with a dementia diagnosis report being told; only ~8% of older Americans with MCI receive a diagnosis. Black (19%) and Hispanic (14%) older adults have higher clinical AD dementia prevalence than White (10%); disparities are attributed largely to social/health factors, not genetics. ~2/3 of people with AD are women.
- **Evidence**: Figures 6, 7; Tables 4, 24; §3.3, §3.8, §3.9 (CHAP, Framingham, Medicare).
- **Implication**: Burden is undercounted and inequitably distributed.

### O7: Biomarkers are redefining "Alzheimer's disease" and expanding the counted population
- **Statement**: 2024 revised criteria stage AD 0-6 and define it biologically. Rough biomarker-based estimates: ~4.5M with dementia due to AD (stages 4-6), ~4.2M with MCI due to AD (stage 3), and ~19M total across stages 1-6 (≈30% of older Americans). First FDA-cleared blood tests (Lumipulse pTau217/Aβ42; Elecsys pTau181) arrived in 2025.
- **Evidence**: Tables 1, 3A-3C; Box 1; §2.1, §3.1; endnote (Jack et al.; Norwegian population study).
- **Implication**: Prevalence estimates depend heavily on clinical-vs-biological definition; biological definitions yield far larger counts.

### O8: Public brain-health knowledge gap (2026 Special Report)
- **Statement**: 99% of adults 40+ view brain health as equally/more important than physical health, but only 9% know "a lot" about how to maintain it, and only 14% have discussed it with their provider. U.S. POINTER showed a structured multidomain lifestyle intervention improved cognition significantly more than a self-guided one.
- **Evidence**: Figures 19-27; Table 25; §8 (U-M National Poll on Healthy Aging, n=3,829; U.S. POINTER, Baker et al.).
- **Implication**: A large opportunity to translate modifiable-risk-factor evidence into accessible, lifespan brain-health programming.

## Gaps

### G1: Clinical-symptom-based counts diverge from biological/administrative counts
- **Statement**: Population prevalence (CHAP, HRS-HCAP) captures more people than health-system diagnosis; biomarker-based prevalence is only beginning to be estimable and uses non-U.S. data.
- **Caused by**: O1, O6, O7.
- **Existing attempts**: CHAP clinical-symptom estimation; HRS-HCAP (adds functional-impairment requirement); a Norwegian biomarker study extrapolated to the U.S.
- **Why they fail**: Single-site geography (CHAP), stricter thresholds miss mild cases (HRS-HCAP), and biomarker data are clinic-based/non-U.S., introducing substantial uncertainty.

### G2: Care demand is outpacing the supply and stability of the workforce
- **Statement**: No single defined dementia care workforce; specialists are scarce and maldistributed (worse in rural areas), and direct-care jobs are underpaid with extreme turnover.
- **Caused by**: O1, O4.
- **Existing attempts**: GWEP, ECHO telementoring, collaborative-care models (GUIDE), community health workers.
- **Why they fail**: Training coverage is incomplete; incentives (pay, loan forgiveness) are insufficient; evidence-based interventions are not yet widespread.

### G3: The cost trajectory threatens public financing
- **Statement**: Costs are concentrated in Medicare/Medicaid and rising toward ~$1T by 2050, with the Medicare HI Trust Fund projected into deficit; new anti-amyloid therapies add cost and access/capacity questions.
- **Caused by**: O1, O5.
- **Existing attempts**: Modeling of delayed-onset/earlier-diagnosis scenarios; ICER value assessments of lecanemab.
- **Why they fail**: Real-world effectiveness, eligibility (only ~8% meet trial criteria in one estimate), and capacity constraints (infusion/PET/biomarker testing) are unresolved.

### G4: Modifiable-risk-factor evidence is not reaching or being acted on by the public
- **Statement**: Strong evidence that ~37-45% of dementia cases are associated with modifiable factors, and U.S. POINTER shows structured lifestyle change protects cognition, yet public knowledge and clinician conversations are rare.
- **Caused by**: O8.
- **Existing attempts**: 10 Healthy Habits, Building Brain-Healthy Habits, Brain Health At Work, ALZPro, HBI Road Map.
- **Why they fail**: Cost/logistics barriers, absence of clinical practice guidelines for brain health, and limited integration into routine care.

## Key Insight
- **Insight**: The public-health impact of AD is defined not only by the disease's biology but by *how it is counted, who provides care, and who pays* — so the report's core contribution is to make the burden legible (national and state-level numbers) and to argue that (a) modifiable risk factors and structured lifestyle intervention (U.S. POINTER) offer a preventive lever across the lifespan, and (b) coordinated care models plus a strengthened, dementia-capable workforce are needed to meet demand.
- **Derived from**: O1-O8.
- **Enables**: Resource allocation, policy (RAISE strategy, GUIDE Model, WA Cares), and a lifespan brain-health agenda.

## Assumptions
- A1: Clinical Alzheimer's dementia can be validly estimated from a single-site longitudinal cohort (CHAP) reweighted to U.S. demographics.
- A2: Death-certificate coding of "Alzheimer's disease," though based on clinical judgment and undercounting, is the standard mortality measure (CDC terminology retained).
- A3: Unpaid caregiving hours can be valued at the average of state minimum wage and median home-health-aide wage (a conservative estimate).
- A4: Per-person cost estimates inflated to 2025/2026 dollars via medical-care CPI components; pre-pandemic (2018-2019) utilization does not reflect post-pandemic shifts.
- A5: Biomarker-based prevalence extrapolations from a non-U.S. (Norwegian) study and clinic-based samples are only rough initial estimates carrying substantial uncertainty.
