# Problem Specification

The report exists to quantify the current and projected public-health, human, and economic burden of Alzheimer's disease (AD) and other dementias in the United States, and to translate that burden into information for the public, clinicians, and policymakers. The 2025 edition adds a Special Report on American attitudes toward early detection and treatment in the new era of disease-modifying therapy.

## Observations

### O1: A large and rapidly growing prevalent population
- **Statement**: An estimated 7.2 million Americans age 65+ live with Alzheimer's dementia in 2025; 74% are age 75 or older; about 1 in 9 (11%) of those 65+ are affected, projected to reach 13.8 million by 2060.
- **Evidence**: §3.2, §3.11.1, Figures 3, 4, 7 (CHAP + U.S. Census projections, Rajan et al.).
- **Implication**: Population aging (58M → 82M people 65+ from 2022 to 2050) will drive absolute case growth even if age-specific incidence declines.

### O2: Rising and under-counted mortality
- **Statement**: 120,122 official AD deaths were recorded in 2022; reported AD deaths rose 142.4% between 2000 and 2022 while deaths from most other leading causes fell; dementia of any type was the underlying cause for 292,881 deaths in 2022.
- **Evidence**: §4.1, §4.3, Figures 8, 9, 10, Table 6 (CDC/NCHS).
- **Implication**: Death-certificate counts substantially undercount true AD-attributable deaths (attributable-mortality studies estimate ~500,000 deaths in those 75+ in 2010).

### O3: A shrinking, strained unpaid caregiver base doing more
- **Statement**: In 2024, ~11.9 million unpaid caregivers provided ~19.2 billion hours of care valued at $413.5 billion; the number of caregivers has declined versus a decade earlier while hours per caregiver rose.
- **Evidence**: §5.1, §5.1.5, Table 10 (BRFSS + Census + NAC/AARP + DOL + Genworth).
- **Implication**: Caregiving imposes documented emotional, physical, and financial harm (e.g., 59% report high/very-high emotional stress; 30-40% depression prevalence).

### O4: Very high and concentrated care costs
- **Statement**: Total 2025 payments for health/long-term/hospice care for people 65+ with dementia are estimated at $384 billion; per-person Medicare payments are ~3x and Medicaid payments ~22x those for beneficiaries without dementia.
- **Evidence**: §7, §7.1, §7.3.4, Figure 15, Table 16 (Lewin Model, MCBS 2018).
- **Implication**: Costs are projected to reach just under $1 trillion by 2050, straining Medicare/Medicaid and family finances.

### O5: A workforce that cannot meet demand
- **Statement**: In 2021 only 7,454 certified geriatricians existed versus ~23,953 needed; ~861,000 additional direct care workers are needed 2022-2032; median direct-care wage was $16.72/hr in 2023.
- **Evidence**: §6.1.2, §6.1.3, Tables 14, 15.
- **Implication**: Without recruitment, training, and pay reform, access to timely diagnosis and quality care will worsen.

### O6: A new treatment era raises the stakes of early detection
- **Statement**: Two anti-amyloid drugs (lecanemab, donanemab) now slow progression in stage 3-4 disease; blood-based biomarker tests are near clinical availability; 99% of surveyed Americans consider early diagnosis important and >9 in 10 would want a simple test.
- **Evidence**: §2.7.1, §8 Special Report, Figures 19-27 (Versta/NORC survey, n=1,702).
- **Implication**: Detection/diagnosis capacity, insurance coverage, and clinician communication must scale to realize the benefit of treatment.

## Gaps

### G1: Death-certificate data cannot measure true dementia mortality
- **Statement**: Cause-of-death coding attributes death to acute conditions (e.g., pneumonia) rather than AD, and 15-30% of clinically diagnosed AD lacks AD brain changes.
- **Caused by**: O2.
- **Existing attempts**: Attributable-risk estimation (Rush MAP/ROS; HRS).
- **Why they fail**: They rely on modeling assumptions and are not routinely available at national/state granularity.

### G2: Symptom-based prevalence differs from biomarker-based disease
- **Statement**: Reported prevalence (7.2M) is symptom-based; biomarker/autopsy studies suggest biomarker-defined prevalence could be up to 30% lower (~5M).
- **Caused by**: O1.
- **Existing attempts**: Autopsy and PET biomarker studies.
- **Why they fail**: No population-based study yet pairs prevalence measurement with biomarker confirmation at scale.

### G3: Under-diagnosis and diagnostic delay, worse for minority groups
- **Statement**: 50%+ of patients are not diagnosed until moderate/advanced stages; delays are longer for Black and Hispanic older adults; only 0.1% of primary-care clinicians detected MCI within the expected range.
- **Caused by**: O5, O6.
- **Existing attempts**: Medicare cognitive-assessment billing code 99483; collaborative care models.
- **Why they fail**: Low uptake (2.4% of beneficiaries with dementia in 2021), workforce shortage, PCP low confidence.

### G4: Public knowledge lags interest, and coverage lags science
- **Statement**: Only 16% of Americans say they know a lot about AD and only 9% know much about biomarker tests, despite ~99% valuing early diagnosis; most states do not require insurers to cover biomarker testing.
- **Caused by**: O6.
- **Existing attempts**: State AIM legislation (16 states require both public+private coverage as of Jan 2025).
- **Why they fail**: Coverage and education have not kept pace with test/treatment availability.

## Key Insight
- **Insight**: The AD burden is best understood as a multi-domain public-health problem — prevalence, mortality, unpaid caregiving, workforce, and cost together — and the arrival of disease-modifying therapy plus near-term blood biomarkers makes *early detection capacity* the pivotal lever that determines whether that burden can be reduced.
- **Derived from**: O1-O6.
- **Enables**: A quantified, state-resolved evidence base to argue for workforce investment, insurance coverage of biomarker testing, and public/clinician education.

## Assumptions
- A1: Symptom-based clinical criteria (CHAP) adequately identify Alzheimer's dementia for headline prevalence, pending biomarker-based population studies.
- A2: Death-certificate underlying-cause coding, while an undercount, is the consistent official basis for mortality statistics.
- A3: Unpaid-care value can be conservatively imputed at the average of the state minimum wage and the median home-health-aide wage.
- A4: Population and cost projections (Census 2024 projections; Lewin Model) hold absent major medical breakthroughs.
