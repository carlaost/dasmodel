# Concepts

Genuine technical terms defined in the 2025 Alzheimer's Disease Facts and Figures report.

## Alzheimer's disease
- **Notation**: AD
- **Definition**: A progressive, ultimately fatal brain disease in which neurons are damaged and destroyed; biologically defined by the accumulation of the protein fragment beta-amyloid (plaques) outside neurons and an abnormal form of the protein tau (tangles) inside neurons, accompanied by inflammation, decreased glucose metabolism, and brain atrophy. Diagnosis "due to Alzheimer's disease" requires imaging, CSF, or other tests indicating these brain changes.
- **Boundary conditions**: The most common cause of dementia (est. 60%-80% of cases), but not the only one; brain changes begin ~20 years before symptoms.
- **Related concepts**: Alzheimer's dementia, Dementia, Biomarkers, Alzheimer's disease continuum

## Dementia
- **Notation**: —
- **Definition**: An overall term for a group of symptoms — trouble with memory, language, and problem-solving; difficulty concentrating; confusion; poor judgment — that result from brain changes and interfere with the ability to perform everyday tasks. Many conditions can cause dementia.
- **Boundary conditions**: A syndrome, not a specific disease; Alzheimer's is one cause among several (Table 1).
- **Related concepts**: Alzheimer's disease, Mixed dementia, Mild cognitive impairment

## Alzheimer's dementia
- **Notation**: —
- **Definition**: Dementia believed to be due to Alzheimer's disease. In this report's Prevalence section, the term is based on studies classifying individuals by clinical symptoms alone (not biomarker-confirmed), because population-based biomarker-confirmed prevalence studies do not yet exist.
- **Boundary conditions**: Distinguished from biomarker-confirmed "dementia due to Alzheimer's disease"; symptom-based prevalence may exceed biomarker-based prevalence by up to ~30%.
- **Related concepts**: Alzheimer's disease, Prevalence, Biomarkers

## Mixed dementia / mixed pathologies
- **Notation**: —
- **Definition**: The presence in one individual of the brain changes of more than one cause of dementia (e.g., Alzheimer's plus cerebrovascular disease). When such pathologies produce dementia symptoms during life, the person is said to have mixed dementia.
- **Boundary conditions**: The norm rather than the exception, especially at age 85+; in one autopsy study only 3% of those with clinical Alzheimer's dementia had AD brain changes alone.
- **Related concepts**: Dementia, Alzheimer's disease, LATE, Lewy body disease

## Alzheimer's disease continuum (clinical staging 0-6)
- **Notation**: Stages 0-6
- **Definition**: The progression of Alzheimer's from unnoticeable brain changes to severe functional impairment, formalized by 2024 revised criteria (Jack et al.) into clinical stages: 0 (asymptomatic, deterministic gene), 1 (asymptomatic, biomarker evidence only), 2 (transitional decline), 3 (cognitive impairment with early functional impact), 4 (dementia, mild functional impairment), 5 (moderate), 6 (severe).
- **Boundary conditions**: Complemented by a biological staging scheme (A-D via PET, Table 3B) and an integrated biological+clinical scheme (Table 3C). Anti-amyloid drugs are approved only for stages 3-4.
- **Related concepts**: Biomarkers, Mild cognitive impairment, Anti-amyloid medications

## Mild cognitive impairment due to Alzheimer's disease
- **Notation**: MCI due to AD
- **Definition**: A symptomatic precursor stage to Alzheimer's dementia in which biomarkers indicate the presence of Alzheimer's brain changes but functional independence is largely retained. Roughly 8%-11% of Americans 65+ (~5-7 million) may have MCI due to AD.
- **Boundary conditions**: ~17%-22% of people 65+ have all-cause MCI; about half of people with MCI have AD-related brain changes.
- **Related concepts**: Alzheimer's disease continuum, Biomarkers, Anti-amyloid medications

## Biomarkers (of Alzheimer's disease)
- **Notation**: beta-amyloid, tau, p-tau (e.g., p-tau217)
- **Definition**: Measurable biological changes that indicate the presence/absence of disease or risk of symptoms. For AD, principally beta-amyloid and phosphorylated tau, measured via PET imaging, cerebrospinal fluid, and (emerging) blood-based tests.
- **Boundary conditions**: Presence of biomarkers alone is not sufficient to diagnose AD; no blood test was FDA-approved for clinical diagnostic use at press time.
- **Related concepts**: Alzheimer's disease, MCI, Early detection

## Anti-amyloid medications (disease-modifying therapy)
- **Notation**: lecanemab (Leqembi), donanemab (Kisunla)
- **Definition**: Monoclonal-antibody drugs that target and remove beta-amyloid from the brain, delaying disease progression in individuals with MCI or mild dementia due to AD (stages 3-4) and confirmed elevated beta-amyloid; administered by intravenous infusion (lecanemab every 2 weeks, donanemab every 4 weeks).
- **Boundary conditions**: Not cures; not established for stages 0-2, 5, or 6; carry risk of amyloid-related imaging abnormalities (ARIA).
- **Related concepts**: ARIA, Biomarkers, Alzheimer's disease continuum

## Amyloid-related imaging abnormalities
- **Notation**: ARIA
- **Definition**: A common side effect of anti-amyloid treatment consisting of typically temporary brain swelling (ARIA-E) and/or small areas of bleeding (ARIA-H), usually asymptomatic but potentially serious; detected via MRI monitoring.
- **Boundary conditions**: Elevated risk in people on anticoagulants, with CAA/ABRA, or carrying two APOE-e4 alleles; managed by temporary/indefinite discontinuation.
- **Related concepts**: Anti-amyloid medications, APOE-e4

## Prevalence vs. incidence
- **Notation**: —
- **Definition**: Prevalence is the number/proportion of people who have a condition at a given time; incidence is the number/rate of new cases per year (often per 100,000). The report distinguishes prevalent burden (7.2M in 2025) from incidence (~910,000 new cases among those 65+ in 2011 per CHAP).
- **Boundary conditions**: Incidence rises steeply with age (0.4% at 65-74, 3.2% at 75-84, 7.6% at 85+ per CHAP 2011).
- **Related concepts**: Alzheimer's dementia, Lifetime risk

## Modifiable risk factors
- **Notation**: —
- **Definition**: Risk factors for cognitive decline/dementia that can be changed. The 2024 Lancet Commission identified 14 (education, vision loss, high cholesterol, head injury, physical inactivity, smoking, excessive alcohol, hypertension, obesity, diabetes, hearing loss, depression, infrequent social contact, air pollution) whose elimination might prevent nearly half of dementia cases worldwide.
- **Boundary conditions**: Associations apply at the population level and cannot guarantee individual outcomes; it is often unclear whether a factor is specific to AD versus other dementias.
- **Related concepts**: Dementia, Cognitive reserve, Social determinants of health
