# Constraints, Assumptions, and Limitations

## Boundary conditions (scope)
- **Geography**: United States only (national and state; some county detail for prevalence).
- **Population**: Primarily adults age 65 and older; younger-onset (<65) is discussed but prevalence data are limited (~200,000 Americans age 30-64).
- **Condition**: "Alzheimer's dementia" in the Prevalence section is symptom-based (clinically diagnosed), not biomarker-confirmed. Where specific AD data are unavailable, the more general "Alzheimer's or other dementias" is used.
- **Currency**: Costs reported in 2024 dollars unless otherwise stated; total-cost figures in 2025 dollars. Data are the most recent finalized available (e.g., mortality through 2022; MCBS 2018; Medicare FFS 2019).
- **Disclaimer (verbatim)**: "The statistics, facts, figures, interpretations, opinions, recommendations and descriptions made in this report are based on currently available data and information as cited in this report, all of which are subject to revision as new data and information become available."

## Key assumptions
- A1: CHAP age/sex/race-specific prevalence rates are applicable nationally when combined with Census projections, despite CHAP being located in a single small geographic area.
- A2: Death-certificate underlying-cause coding is the consistent official basis for mortality, accepted as an undercount.
- A3: Unpaid-care hours are valued conservatively at the average of the state minimum wage and the median home-health-aide hourly cost.
- A4: Workforce need assumes 30% of adults 65+ have complex needs benefiting from a geriatrician, each geriatrician serving up to 700 patients, and Alzheimer's prevalence of ~11% of older adults across all states.
- A5: Cost projections (Lewin Model) and prevalence projections (CHAP/Census) assume no major preventive or curative breakthrough.
- A6: State death rates are presented unadjusted (not age-adjusted) to depict absolute burden (endnote A6).

## Known limitations (stated in the report)
- **L1 — Symptom-based vs biomarker-based prevalence**: Reported 7.2M is symptom-based; biomarker/autopsy studies suggest 15-30% of clinically diagnosed AD lacks AD brain changes, so biomarker-defined prevalence could be up to 30% lower (~5M). No population-based biomarker-confirmed prevalence study yet exists.
- **L2 — Single-site prevalence source**: CHAP is a single, small geographic cohort and may not be nationally representative; alternative estimates (e.g., Brookmeyer et al.) are lower but statistically consistent.
- **L3 — Estimate divergence across studies**: CHAP (6.07M AD-only) vs HRS-HCAP (4.92M all-cause) differ because of differing dementia ascertainment (HRS-HCAP requires informant-reported functional impairment).
- **L4 — Mortality undercount**: Death certificates undercount AD-attributable deaths; acute conditions (pneumonia) are often listed instead of AD.
- **L5 — DALY/disability estimates**: Global Burden of Disease disability estimates use differing state data sources across years and do not account for social/economic context, limiting comparability.
- **L6 — Cost-model dependence**: Total and projected costs depend on the Lewin Model and 2018 MCBS / 2019 Medicare FFS tabulations; utilization data largely predate the COVID-19 pandemic, whose long-term effect on patterns is unclear.
- **L7 — Caregiving value is conservative**: The imputed dollar value uses a low wage benchmark and may not capture home modifications, respite, or lost productivity.
- **L8 — Survey scope (Special Report)**: The attitudes survey covers U.S. adults age 45+ (not all adults), n=1,702; findings are self-reported and weighted to Census totals but subject to sampling and social-desirability limitations.
- **L9 — Treatment evidence horizon**: Long-term (beyond trial duration) effectiveness of lecanemab/donanemab is not yet established; short-term benefit may be imperceptible to individuals.
