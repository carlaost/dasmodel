## Trend summary
The actual (red) dementia share rises steadily and substantially across the sorted country order, from roughly 3.9% (Sweden) to roughly 18.1% (Portugal) — a range of about 14 percentage points. The counterfactual (grey) share — what dementia prevalence would be in each country if its population had the average level of childhood education across all 28 units instead of its own — is comparatively flat, clustering mostly in the ≈8.0–9.4% band across nearly all countries, with Spain (≈10.7%) as a visible outlier on the high side. This large compression of the counterfactual range relative to the actual range is the figure's central visual argument: most of the actual cross-national variation in dementia prevalence disappears once countries are equalized on education, consistent with the paper's claim that education differences are the dominant driver of the observed international variation (§Results: "This variation is dramatically smaller than the actual variation, showing the strength of the association between education and the probability of being demented").

Note: these digitized bar values are the ARA's own visual readings of Figure 2 and are not claimed to reproduce Table 3's HCAP-validated Demented,% column exactly — visual comparison suggests the two are related but not numerically identical for several countries (e.g., Sweden reads ≈3.9% here vs. 5.0% in Table 3; Austria ≈4.5% here vs. 6.8% in Table 3), which may reflect a different regression specification, covariate-mean evaluation, or rounding used specifically for this figure. This discrepancy is not resolved by the paper text and is flagged rather than silently reconciled (see `logic/solution/constraints.md`).
