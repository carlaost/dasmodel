# Problem Specification

## Observations

### O1: Multiple amyloid-targeting therapies reduce amyloid but cannot be compared head-to-head
- **Statement**: Aducanumab, donanemab, and lecanemab each target different Aβ epitopes/forms and have all reached regulatory approval (aducanumab 2021, lecanemab 2023, donanemab 2024), but comparing their amyloid-lowering results across separate trials is confounded by differences in study populations and trial designs.
- **Evidence**: §1 (Background); Research in Context (§ box), point 1.
- **Implication**: Indirect cross-trial comparison of amyloid outcomes is "not advised"; a direct active-comparator trial is needed.

### O2: Donanemab and aducanumab have distinct mechanisms
- **Statement**: Aducanumab targets aggregated Aβ (soluble oligomers and insoluble fibrils); donanemab is an IgG1 mAb that selectively binds insoluble N-terminal truncated pyroglutamate Aβ (N3pG) present only in mature plaques, cleared via microglial phagocytosis.
- **Evidence**: §1 (Background); refs 8, 12.
- **Implication**: Mechanistic differences motivate a comparison of the depth and speed of plaque clearance.

### O3: Donanemab previously slowed clinical decline via amyloid reduction
- **Statement**: In TRAILBLAZER-ALZ 2 (phase 3), donanemab reduced brain amyloid plaque and slowed clinical progression at 18 months, replicating the phase 2 TRAILBLAZER-ALZ result.
- **Evidence**: §1; refs 13, 14.
- **Implication**: Establishes plausibility of donanemab efficacy and the low–medium tau subpopulation framing.

### O4: Aducanumab was expected to become standard of care but was later discontinued
- **Statement**: At trial conception, aducanumab was anticipated to become standard of care for early symptomatic AD; in January 2024 its manufacturer discontinued development and commercialization globally.
- **Evidence**: §1; §4 (Discussion); refs 10, 11.
- **Implication**: The comparison's clinical relevance is now uncertain, but the trial provides the only direct ATT-vs-ATT biomarker comparison.

## Gaps

### G1: No direct comparator trial of two amyloid-targeting therapies
- **Statement**: Before this study, no randomized trial had directly compared two ATTs on amyloid plaque clearance.
- **Caused by**: O1, O2.
- **Existing attempts**: Indirect/cross-trial comparisons.
- **Why they fail**: Population and design heterogeneity make indirect comparisons unreliable.

### G2: Relationship between speed/depth of amyloid clearance and ARIA risk unknown
- **Statement**: Whether faster or deeper amyloid removal increases the frequency/risk of amyloid-related imaging abnormalities (ARIA) was unclear.
- **Caused by**: O2 (mechanistic differences in clearance kinetics).
- **Existing attempts**: ARIA characterized within single-agent trials.
- **Why they fail**: No within-trial contrast of two clearance profiles under identical MRI schedules.

## Key Insight
- **Insight**: A randomized, open-label, active-comparator trial with identical florbetapir-PET clearance endpoints and identical safety-MRI schedules can directly test superiority of donanemab vs aducanumab on amyloid plaque clearance while simultaneously decoupling clearance kinetics from ARIA risk.
- **Derived from**: O1, O2, G1, G2.
- **Enables**: The two co-primary clearance endpoints (overall + low–medium tau) plus secondary time-to-clearance, absolute/percent lowering, biomarkers, and ARIA analyses.

## Assumptions
- A1: Amyloid plaque clearance is defined as florbetapir PET < 24.1 Centiloids (refs 17, 18).
- A2: The low–medium tau subpopulation (defined by flortaucipir PET) is a meaningful stratum, ~50% of enrollees (per power assumptions).
- A3: Centrally blinded reads of MRI and amyloid PET mitigate the open-label design's bias for imaging endpoints.
- A4: Aducanumab dosing per US label (ADUHELM) is the appropriate comparator regimen.
