# Problem Specification

## Observations

### O1: Dementia is a large and growing burden in Europe
- **Statement**: Nearly 14 million people already live with dementia in the European Union alone; prevalence is anticipated to double by 2050, following improvements in life expectancy and medical treatment.
- **Evidence**: §Introduction ("In the European Union alone, nearly 14 million people already live with dementia [3]... Since the prevalence of dementia is growing steadily and is anticipated to double by 2050 [3], following the improvement in life expectancy and overall medical treatments [8]").
- **Implication**: The scale and trajectory of dementia prevalence puts sustained pressure on European national health systems, motivating better understanding of access/utilization barriers.

### O2: Dementia care is economically costly
- **Statement**: Dementia care costs an estimated 392 billion EUR per year in Europe, with a cost per person of circa 27,815 EUR.
- **Evidence**: §Introduction ("It is estimated that dementia care costs 392 billion EUR per year in Europe, with a cost per person of circa 27,815 [3]").
- **Implication**: The cost burden on families (direct out-of-pocket and informal care costs) and national health systems makes improving service utilization/adherence a financial as well as clinical priority.

### O3: Service utilization remains suboptimal despite available formal support
- **Statement**: Despite the availability of formal support systems and healthcare workers, utilization of dementia care services is still partial, particularly for community services such as home support, day care, respite care, and counselling.
- **Evidence**: §Introduction ("Despite the availability of support services, their utilization is still partial, particularly for community services such as home support, day care, respite care, and counselling [9]").
- **Implication**: Several factors drive low utilization: non-compliance with care/medication schedules, lack of caregiver awareness/knowledge, unfamiliarity with services, time constraints, limited service availability, poor care coordination, and unclear access procedures.

### O4: Primary care management of dementia is suboptimal and under-resourced
- **Statement**: Dementia management in primary care remains suboptimal; primary care physicians (PCPs), often the first medical contact for people with cognitive problems and caregivers, face insufficient training, limited consultation time, and a lack of comprehensive guidelines.
- **Evidence**: §Introduction ("Overall, dementia management in primary care remains suboptimal [21]... PCPs are often the first medical contact... but face several challenges... such as insufficient training, limited consultation time, and a lack of comprehensive guidelines [11, 23–25]").
- **Implication**: Structural gaps at the primary-care entry point compound downstream barriers to specialised dementia services.

### O5: COVID-19 exacerbated existing care barriers
- **Statement**: The COVID-19 pandemic caused substantial disruptions in healthcare systems, further exacerbating symptoms in people living with dementia (increased stress, behavioral problems, social isolation, depressive symptoms) and increasing caregiver burden and psychological distress.
- **Evidence**: §Introduction ("HCWs faced significant challenges during the COVID-19 pandemic, which caused substantial disruptions in healthcare systems [26, 27]. These disruptions have further exacerbated symptoms in people living with dementia... [28, 29], and increasing the care burden and psychological distress for family caregivers [30–32]").
- **Implication**: Barrier identification must account for pandemic-era disruptions, several of which were captured directly in the included literature (8 studies published in 2020, 8 in 2021).

## Gaps

### G1: No prior synthesis of dementia care access/utilization barriers specific to Europe
- **Statement**: To the authors' knowledge, this is the first review summarising the main barriers to accessing and utilising dementia care services specifically within Europe.
- **Caused by**: O1, O2, O3, O4
- **Existing attempts**: Individual primary studies (qualitative interviews, focus groups, surveys) on barriers in single countries or small multi-country projects (e.g., Actifcare, ICARE4EU), and non-European or narrower prior reviews (e.g., refs 36–38 on advance care planning, family-physician/case-manager collaboration, and ethnic-minority accessibility).
- **Why they fail**: These are single-country, single-population, or narrowly-scoped (e.g., ethnicity-only, ACP-only) syntheses; none provides a Europe-wide, PRISMA-guided synthesis spanning PLWD, caregivers, and HCWs across all barrier types simultaneously.

### G2: Barriers are fragmented across disconnected literatures and populations
- **Statement**: Individual barrier types (organisational, informational, cultural/stigma, financial, logistical) have been documented in disparate single-population or single-setting studies, but not consolidated into a single categorisation applicable across care settings and stakeholder groups.
- **Caused by**: O3, O4
- **Existing attempts**: 29 primary studies each document one or more barrier types in a specific country/setting/population combination (Table 3).
- **Why they fail**: Without consolidation, policymakers lack a systems-level view of which barrier categories dominate, and in which settings/populations, across Europe.

### G3: Minority/migrant-specific barriers are under-addressed and under-reported
- **Statement**: Most studies did not specify race/ethnicity of participants, limiting understanding of barriers across different racial and ethnic groups, even though some studies did investigate specific minority-group barriers (Sikh caregivers in the UK, Bangladeshi communities in England, Middle Eastern/Eastern European/Pakistani minorities in Denmark, Somali/Pakistani/Turkish/Polish/Croatian/Indian minorities in Norway).
- **Caused by**: O3
- **Existing attempts**: A handful of studies (refs 41, 50, 53, 54) specifically address minority/migrant populations.
- **Why they fail**: The general absence of ethnicity reporting across most of the 29 studies limits the ability to generalise minority-specific barrier findings.

## Key Insight
- **Insight**: Barriers to dementia care access and utilization in Europe are not a single undifferentiated problem but decompose into (at least) five recurring, cross-cutting thematic domains — organisational, informational/educational, cultural/stigma-related, financial, and logistical — that recur across different countries, care settings (home-based, primary, hospital, residential/long-term, community), and stakeholder groups (PLWD, caregivers, HCWs), with organisational barriers (poor coordination, unclear procedures) being the most frequently reported domain (n=17 of 29 studies).
- **Derived from**: O3, O4, G2
- **Enables**: A structured, PRISMA/PICO-based systematic review and narrative synthesis that maps barrier domains onto studies, countries, and populations, providing policymakers a basis for tailored, region-specific interventions rather than one-size-fits-all recommendations.

## Assumptions
- A1: Only original research (cohort, cross-sectional, case-control, or qualitative studies) conducted in European settings and published in English, from 2013 onward, is eligible — randomized controlled trials, case-control studies, editorials, letters, and conference papers are deliberately excluded a priori (a fundamental review-design choice, not an oversight).
- A2: A study qualifies if it addresses at least one barrier related to access to dementia care, regardless of the age, gender, race, or socioeconomic status of the population studied (PLWD with formal diagnosis, and/or formal/informal caregivers, and/or HCWs/stakeholders).
- A3: Grey literature, conference papers, dissertations, letters, and editorials are excluded from consideration.
- A4: MMAT quality scores below the 50% threshold do not automatically exclude a study; such studies are retained but their findings are interpreted with caution regarding reliability.
- A5: Barriers related to assistive technologies are deliberately excluded from this iteration of the review, deferred to a dedicated future review given their scope and growing significance (accelerated by COVID-19).
- A6: The review does not assess the effectiveness of strategies already in place to address the identified barriers — it is scoped to barrier identification, not intervention evaluation.
