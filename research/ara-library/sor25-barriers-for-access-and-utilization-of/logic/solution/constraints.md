# Constraints, Assumptions, and Limitations

## Scope boundaries (what the review does and does not cover)
- **Population**: People formally diagnosed with dementia or Alzheimer's disease, their formal and/or informal caregivers, and/or healthcare professionals/other stakeholders involved in dementia care, regardless of age, gender, race, or socioeconomic status.
- **Geography**: European settings only. Studies conducted outside Europe were excluded even if otherwise eligible (4 of the 44 full-text exclusions were for this reason).
- **Study types included**: Original research only — cohort studies, cross-sectional studies, case-control studies, or qualitative investigations.
- **Study types excluded**: Randomized controlled trials (RCTs), case-control studies (as an excluded "other study type" category per Table 1's exclusion criteria), editorials, letters, conference papers. Grey literature, dissertations were also excluded.
- **Language**: English only.
- **Timeframe**: Studies published in or after 2013 (search window 2013–2023 per the PICO Timeframe field; included studies span 2013–2022).
- **Barrier scope**: The review covers barriers to *access and utilization* of dementia care services; it explicitly excludes barriers related to assistive technologies (deferred to a dedicated future review) and does not assess the *effectiveness* of existing strategies to address the identified barriers.
- **Comparison**: No comparator arm — the PICO "Comparison" field is "Not applicable," consistent with a barrier-identification (not intervention-effectiveness) review.

## Assumptions
- A1: MMAT quality scores below the 50% threshold do not trigger automatic exclusion; such studies are retained and interpreted with added caution regarding reliability.
- A2: In mixed-methods studies, overall quality cannot exceed the score of the weakest methodological component (an MMAT scoring convention adopted by the review).
- A3: A single study can be assigned to multiple barrier categories simultaneously; categories are not mutually exclusive.
- A4: Excluding RCTs was a deliberate a priori choice to prioritize in-depth, real-world, context-specific barrier accounts over intervention-testing designs, even though this is acknowledged as "a fundamental limitation of this review."
- A5: Combining findings from multiple, heterogeneous study designs (qualitative, quantitative, mixed) is done via narrative synthesis rather than meta-analysis, given the non-quantitative, non-homogeneous nature of "barrier" outcomes across studies.

## Known limitations (as stated by the authors)
- **Geographic/cultural generalizability**: Exclusive focus on European settings restricts generalizability of findings to non-European contexts where healthcare structures, cultural attitudes, and resource allocation may differ significantly.
- **Setting conflation**: Multiple studies focused on more than one care setting and often did not differentiate results by setting, making it difficult to isolate barriers specific to particular care environments.
- **Ethnicity/race under-reporting**: Most studies did not specify participants' race/ethnicity, limiting understanding of barriers across different racial/ethnic groups — although a subset of studies did specifically investigate minority-group barriers (see `logic/claims.md` C10).
- **RCT exclusion**: A "fundamental limitation" — RCTs, with their focus on testing interventions under controlled conditions, may not fully capture the complex, context-specific barriers this review targets; the authors defend this as a deliberate scope choice aligned with the research objective, while noting future work could combine qualitative and quantitative approaches.
- **No intervention-effectiveness assessment**: The review identifies and analyses barriers but does not assess the effectiveness of existing strategies already addressing them; the authors flag this as future research.
- **Assistive-technology barriers excluded**: Acknowledged as growing in significance (especially post-COVID-19) but out of scope, deferred to a dedicated future review due to topic complexity/scope.
- **Language/publication bias**: Restriction to English-language studies may introduce language and geographical biases, potentially overlooking valuable non-English literature; reliance on published literature may also introduce publication bias (positive-result studies more likely to be published).
- **Internal source inconsistencies noted during ARA compilation** (not authors' stated limitations, but observed during grounding): (1) the Abstract states "Over 1298 articles" while the Results/Fig. 1 pipeline reports 1748 database-identified + 4 reference-mined records and 1202 unique records after deduplication — these numbers do not reconcile within the source text; (2) the cultural/stigma-related barrier category is stated as n=10 but only 7 citation numbers are listed immediately after it in the Results text. Both are reported in `logic/claims.md` (C01, C04) rather than silently corrected.

## Strengths (as stated by the authors)
- Follows PRISMA guidelines for transparent, rigorous, bias-minimizing review process.
- Comprehensive search strategy across five databases (PubMed/MEDLINE, Embase, PsycINFO, HTA Database, Web of Science) over a ten-year window.
- Multiple independent reviewers (five for screening/extraction, one senior reviewer for conflict resolution) enhance reliability of study selection.
- Systematic methodological quality assessment (MMAT) of all included studies.
- Rayyan AI-assisted screening improved efficiency and consistency of title/abstract review at scale.
- Diverse study populations (PLWD, caregivers, HCWs) analysed both collectively and separately across the included studies.
