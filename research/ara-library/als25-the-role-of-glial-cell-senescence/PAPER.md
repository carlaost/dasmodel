# The Role of Glial Cell Senescence in Alzheimer's Disease

## Overview

This is a **narrative review** (Journal of Neurochemistry, 2025) synthesizing evidence that
cellular senescence in glial cells — astrocytes, microglia, and oligodendrocytes/oligodendrocyte
precursor cells (OPCs) — is a driver of Alzheimer's disease (AD) pathogenesis, not merely a
neuronal bystander phenomenon. The authors assemble a mechanistic framework in which aging- and
pathology-related stressors (mitochondrial dysfunction, telomere erosion, oncogene activation, DNA
damage, oxidative stress) trigger permanent growth arrest and a senescence-associated secretory
phenotype (SASP) in glia; the resulting chronic neuroinflammation, impaired phagocytic clearance
of Aβ/tau, and remyelination failure feed positive feedback loops that accelerate neurodegeneration
and cognitive decline. The review catalogues cell-type-specific senescence markers in normal aging
(Table 1) and in AD/pathology-induced contexts (Table 2), and surveys emerging senotherapeutics
(senolytics and senomorphics), their preclinical efficacy, early human trial results, and their
limitations. No original data, code, or datasets are produced; the artifact reconstructs the
review's claims, mechanistic model, concept vocabulary, and citation dependency graph.
