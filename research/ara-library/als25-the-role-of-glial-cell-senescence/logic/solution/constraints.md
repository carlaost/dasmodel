# Constraints, Assumptions, and Limitations

*Grounding: full-text, primarily §1.3 and §2 (Conclusions). These are limitations the review
explicitly states about the glial-senescence hypothesis and about senotherapeutics.*

## Boundary conditions of the hypothesis
- Senescence is a **complex, heterogeneous** process; marker expression varies significantly by cell type, brain region, and pathological context.
- The framework applies to proliferation-competent glia (astrocytes, microglia, OPCs) via replicative and stress-induced senescence. **Mature oligodendrocytes and neurons are post-mitotic**, so any senescent-like state must arise through division-independent mechanisms.
- Findings derive largely from rodent models and in vitro cultures; extrapolation to human AD is assumed, not proven, for many mechanistic steps.

## Known limitations of the evidence
- **L1 — Marker non-specificity and non-universality**: Common markers (SA-β-gal, p16INK4A, p21WAF1/CIP1) are often nonspecific or low-sensitivity; senescent cells are hard to distinguish from quiescent or terminally differentiated cells. Some markers (HMGB1) have been linked to only a single cell type so far. Mouse models show higher p16INK4A/p21WAF1/CIP1 in microglia and OPCs than astrocytes (Ogrodnik 2021). (See claim C09, gap G3.)
- **L2 — Mature-oligodendrocyte senescence unproven in AD**: Direct evidence of senescence in mature oligodendrocytes has not yet been reported in AD or other neurodegenerative conditions (gap G2). The authors argue it "should not be ruled out" and call for targeted study.
- **L3 — Cell-type-specific mechanisms incompletely understood**: The specific mechanisms by which senescence affects each brain cell type in AD pathogenesis are "not yet fully understood" (gap G1).
- **L4 — Unknown normal→pathological transition**: The key factors driving the transition from normal brain aging to pathological aging remain unknown; it is unclear whether AD pathology exacerbates pre-existing aging senescence.

## Limitations of senotherapeutics (§1.3)
- **L5 — Non-universal efficacy/potency**: Effects vary across cell types and models given diverse senescence triggers; e.g., dasatinib eliminated senescent preadipocytes but not human endothelial cells, whereas quercetin showed the opposite (Zhu 2015).
- **L6 — Microenvironment dependence**: Oxygen levels, circulating SASP components, and nutrient availability influence senescence; caloric restriction is protective, so **dietary factors could modulate senotherapeutic efficacy** and should be integrated into clinical trial design.
- **L7 — Systemic exposure and off-target toxicity**: Senescence is systemic (whole-body, not brain-confined); local brain delivery is impractical, so systemic effects are unavoidable. In aged individuals, senescence triggers (mitochondrial/protein-degradation deficits) are often already compromised in not-yet-senescent cells, raising concern that senolytics could disrupt homeostasis and cause generalized cytotoxicity.
- **L8 — Elimination of beneficial senescence**: Senescence supports wound healing and tumor suppression; indiscriminate removal of senescent cells may cause unforeseen harm. Large-scale removal of senescent cells accumulated in the aging brain may itself be detrimental.
- **L9 — Unknown long-term efficacy**: Long-term efficacy of senolytics is largely unexplored; intermittent "hit-and-run" dosing is proposed to minimize off-target effects but needs validation.
- **L10 — Continuous dosing for senomorphics**: Unlike senolytics, senomorphics require continuous treatment to maintain SASP/inflammation suppression.
- **L11 — Heterogeneity challenge for targeting**: Heterogeneity of triggers, cell types, and extracellular environments makes developing subpopulation-specific senolytics a major challenge; personalized senolytic + senomorphic combinations may be required.
- **L12 — No human clinical efficacy yet**: In symptomatic early AD, D+Q reduced SASP factors and reached CSF but showed no effect on cognition or structural MRI in the short study period (Gonzales 2023); large randomized controlled trials are still needed.

## Assumptions carried into the ARA
- A1: Multi-marker panels validly identify senescent glia (acknowledged imperfect — L1).
- A2: Rodent and in vitro findings inform human AD.
- A3: Marker correlation + clearance/rescue experiments jointly imply causality.
- A4: Senescence across neurodegenerative diseases (PD, ALS, HD) reflects a common feature of neurodegeneration.
