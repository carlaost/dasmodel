# Constraints, Assumptions, and Limitations

## Scope boundaries (what the paper does and does not cover)
- **Data source**: Secondary analysis of GBD 2021 estimates only; no new primary data (surveys, clinical assessments, biomarkers) were collected.
- **Outcome measures**: Incidence, mortality (deaths), and DALYs for ADOD, plus their age-standardized rates (ASIR, ASDR, ASR-DALYs), 1990-2021. **Prevalence is explicitly excluded** — "the study's focus on estimated mortality and incidence rates of Alzheimer's disease and other dementias, derived through joint modeling in the GBD study, means prevalence considerations were not included in this analysis" (§Discussion "Limitations").
- **Risk factors**: Only three attributable risk factors are analyzed — smoking, high fasting plasma glucose (FPG), and high body mass index (BMI) — "due to the availability of data, this study only focused on three risk factors... not all potential risk factors for ADOD were exhaustively considered" (§Discussion "Limitations").
- **Geography**: 204 countries/territories, grouped into 21 GBD regions and, in parallel, 5 SDI quintiles; both grouping schemes are reported but neither supersedes the other.
- **Population**: Both sexes combined and sex-stratified (male, female) estimates; all ages combined (no age-stratified breakdown reported in the main text beyond the composite ASR).
- **Statistical scope**: Joinpoint regression (temporal trend segmentation), restricted cubic splines (SDI-burden association, 21-region/multi-year level), and quantile regression (SDI-burden association, 204-country/2021 cross-section) — no causal-inference method (e.g., instrumental variables, natural experiment) is used; all reported associations are descriptive/correlational.
- **Ethics**: Ethical approval was deemed not applicable, since all GBD 2021 data sources are publicly listed at the Global Health Data Exchange website.

## Assumptions
- A1: GBD 2021 estimates (incidence, mortality, DALYs, risk-factor attribution) are accepted as ground truth for the purposes of this secondary analysis, with all attendant GBD-methodology limitations inherited (see "Known limitations" below).
- A2: SDI quintile boundaries are fixed at: high SDI (0.8103-1.000), high-middle SDI (0.7120-0.8103), middle SDI (0.6188-0.7120), low-middle SDI (0.4658-0.6188), low SDI (0-0.4658).
- A3: PAF risk-factor decomposition assumes that "exposure levels of other factors remain unchanged" when estimating the burden attributable to any single risk factor (smoking, high FPG, high BMI) — i.e., risk factors are treated independently for attribution purposes, not jointly/interactively.
- A4: Joinpoint-regression trend classification (upward/downward/stable) requires both the point estimate and both 95% CI limits to share the same sign; otherwise the segment is classified as stable.
- A5: The restricted cubic spline model uses dummy variables for outlier regions specifically to prevent those regions from skewing the average relationship being estimated for each SDI group.
- A6: Quantile regression was chosen over standard linear regression specifically because it can reveal whether the SDI-burden relationship differs across the distribution of country-level rates, not only at the mean.

## Known limitations (as stated by the authors)
- **GBD-methodology-inherited limitations**: "The methods employed to estimate missing data and adjust for measurement differences among source studies, while valuable, do not substitute for high-quality surveillance data from every country, utilizing standardized case definitions and measurement methods. The accuracy of ADOD estimates may be affected by parameters and models, particularly in certain countries." (§Discussion "Limitations")
- **Prevalence excluded**: The analysis covers incidence, mortality, and DALYs only; prevalence trends are not assessed.
- **Incomplete risk-factor coverage**: Only smoking, high BMI, and high FPG were analyzed; "not all potential risk factors for ADOD were exhaustively considered," due to GBD data availability constraints.
- **Data-quality heterogeneity across countries**: GBD's reliance on available data sources, with continuous method refinement across iterations, "may still encounter limitations in generating completely realistic results for international comparison," particularly affecting lower-data-availability countries/regions (e.g., much of Sub-Saharan Africa, where the paper separately notes "limited productivity and lack of standardized treatment and diagnosis may lead to unequal access to healthcare... resulting in poorer outcomes and increased disease burden").
- **Supplementary-material dependency**: Several results discussed in the main text (Table S1, Table S3, Figure S1-S7 — death/DALY breakdowns by sex and region, and additional spline/heatmap plots) are reported only in a separate Supplementary Material file that was not provided as input to this ARA compilation; those specific figures are cited from the main-text prose (grounded, but not independently re-verified against their own source table/figure) — see `logic/claims.md` C01, C05, C08, C12, C13 and `evidence/README.md`.
- **Possible internal source inconsistency (observed during ARA compilation, not an authors' stated limitation)**: The Discussion states Central Sub-Saharan Africa's 1990-2021 mortality ETPC as 14.82%, a figure that numerically coincides with Australasia's (unrelated) 1990-2021 *incidence*-ASIR ETPC of −14.82(−19.55,−10.2) in Table 1 — Central Sub-Saharan Africa's own incidence ETPC in Table 1 is −0.59(−3.02,1.79). Because the cited mortality figure's source table (Table S1) is not provided with this input, this is reported as an observed coincidence, not a confirmed transcription error — see `logic/claims.md` C13.

## Strengths (as stated by the authors / evident from the design)
- Uses the most recent GBD data vintage (GBD 2021), updating prior GBD-2016/GBD-2019-based dementia burden and risk-factor analyses (refs 20, 21).
- Combines three complementary statistical lenses on the SDI-burden relationship: joinpoint regression (temporal trend segmentation), restricted cubic splines (region-year panel, non-linear mean association), and quantile regression (204-country cross-section, distributional association) — rather than relying on a single linear-trend or linear-correlation method.
- Sex-stratified throughout (both sexes, male, female) for every reported outcome and risk factor, directly addressing the paper's motivating observation that ADOD disproportionately affects women.
- Reports results at three nested geographic resolutions in parallel: global, SDI quintile / GBD super-region / GBD region (21 regions), and individual country (204 countries) — enabling both broad-pattern and country-specific findings (e.g., identifying China, the USA, and India as the top three incident-case countries).
