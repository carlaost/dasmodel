Evidence from clinical and preclinical studies implicates brain microcirculation, cerebral hypoperfusion, blood–brain barrier dysfunction, and reduced amyloid clearance in Alzheimer pathophysiology. ## Overview

This 2025 review argues that Alzheimer disease should be understood through dysregulation of the brain microcirculation, blood-brain barrier, neurovascular unit, and fluid-circulation systems. The abstract frames stable neuronal signaling as dependent on central nervous system barriers that maintain the brain microenvironment and separate it from potentially neurotoxic blood contents.

The review is described as having two parts: a description of healthy cerebral microcirculation, blood flow, extracellular fluid drainage, and neurovascular-unit components with emphasis on the blood-brain barrier, followed by a summary of how those systems are altered in Alzheimer disease. Its main synthesis is that aberrant pericytes are early and central players in Alzheimer pathophysiology, with roles in barrier integrity, capillary flow and stalling, neurovascular coupling, drainage, amyloid and tau clearance, receptor-mediated vasoactivity, apolipoprotein E isoform-specific transport, hypoperfusion, PDGBB-PDGFRbeta signaling, therapeutic targeting, and regenerative therapy.
