## Trend summary
- In community care, both the minimum and maximum weekly caregiving hours generally rise as severity increases: Mild (1–83 h/week, n=23) → Moderate (3–68 h/week, n=27) → Severe (21–120 h/week, n=25). The Moderate max (68 h/week) is slightly lower than the Mild max (83 h/week), but the Moderate minimum (3 h/week) and especially the Severe minimum (21 h/week) rise substantially, so the overall range shifts upward with severity — consistent with the main text's exact statement (quoted in C07) that "caregiving hours were reported to increase by at least 1.4× from mild to more severe stages in community care settings."
- The community-care MCI bucket (12–21 h/week, n=2) sits within the Mild bucket's range but is based on only 2 studies — the sparsest evidence base of any bucket in the figure, consistent with problem.md G3 (MCI-stage evidence scarcity).
- In institutional care, hours rise from Moderate (3–69 h/week, n=5) to Severe (5–97 h/week, n=5) — both the minimum and maximum increase, matching C07's exact statement that institutional-care "informal caregiving hours per week also increased from up to 69 h per week in moderate dementia stages to up to 97 h per week in severe dementia stages."
- At the Severe stage, the institutional-care maximum (97 h/week) is lower than the community-care maximum (120 h/week), but institutional-care studies still show substantial informal (family-provided, not paid-staff) caregiving hours even though the patient is formally institutionalized — supporting the paper's broader point (Results, p.1813) that formal/institutional care does not eliminate informal caregiving burden.
