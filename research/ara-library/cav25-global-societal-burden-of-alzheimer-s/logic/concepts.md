# Concepts

## Targeted Literature Review (TLR)
- **Notation**: —
- **Definition**: A literature review, distinct from a full systematic literature review (SLR), that uses a defined but non-exhaustive search strategy (here, following PRISMA-S guidelines across MEDLINE/Embase plus hand-searched grey literature) targeted at answering specific research questions rather than exhaustively cataloguing all literature on a topic.
- **Boundary conditions**: This paper's TLR combined an AD-specific database search (n=3531 hits) with a broader-dementia secondary search (n=168 hits) and grey-literature hand search (websites, organisations, citation searching), screened via PICOS criteria (Table 1), single-reviewer with second-reviewer QA.
- **Related concepts**: PICOS criteria, Systematic Literature Review (SLR), PRISMA flow diagram

## PICOS Criteria
- **Notation**: —
- **Definition**: Population, Intervention, Comparison, Outcome, and Study design criteria used to define article inclusion/exclusion for the review, detailed in Table 1.
- **Boundary conditions**: Population restricted to adult patients with MCI due to AD or AD/dementia with a defined severity (mild/moderate/severe/moderately-severe-to-severe); no intervention/comparator applicable; outcomes limited to direct/indirect/societal costs, caregiver profiles/hours, and non-medical costs not typically reported; study designs limited to cost-of-illness, real-world (prospective/retrospective/cross-sectional), and systematic reviews/meta-analyses; English language only; 2013–2024 timeframe (individual pre-2013 studies admissible only via inclusion in an eligible SLR).
- **Related concepts**: Targeted Literature Review (TLR)

## Disease Severity Staging Instrument
- **Notation**: —
- **Definition**: An umbrella term for the clinical rating scales used across the 81 reviewed studies to classify AD/dementia severity, harmonized by the review authors into four buckets (MCI/very mild, mild, moderate, severe) regardless of the specific instrument used.
- **Boundary conditions**: The five instruments named in the paper are CDR-SB, FAST, MMSE, GDS, and DS (see individual entries below); MMSE was most common (n=38 studies), followed by CDR (n=6).
- **Related concepts**: CDR-SB, FAST, MMSE, GDS, Dependence Scale (DS)

## Clinical Dementia Rating – Sum of Boxes (CDR-SB)
- **Notation**: —
- **Definition**: "Widely used in research settings," assesses "the cognitive and functional capability of the individual" (Introduction, p.1799).
- **Boundary conditions**: The Discussion notes CDR-based studies show greater fold-change in cost progression from mild-to-severe than MMSE-based studies, and that CDR "may be more sensitive towards the earlier disease stages (e.g., MCI due to AD and mild AD)" than MMSE.
- **Related concepts**: FAST, MMSE, Disease Severity Staging Instrument

## Functional Assessment Staging Test (FAST)
- **Notation**: —
- **Definition**: "Primarily assesses the functional decline of the individual, based on their ability to perform BADL and iADL" (Introduction, p.1799); comprised of seven stages from normal (stage 1) to severe Alzheimer's dementia (stage 7) (Discussion, p.1817).
- **Boundary conditions**: The Discussion attributes one anomalous finding (a Brazilian study showing higher costs in milder than more severe stages) to the FAST scale's dependence-based severity definition being "not entirely comparable to studies that assessed disease severity based on MMSE or CDR scoring."
- **Related concepts**: CDR-SB, Dependence Scale (DS)

## Mini-Mental State Examination (MMSE)
- **Notation**: —
- **Definition**: "Widely used in both clinical trials and in clinical settings," "solely focusses on cognition, evaluating the state of one's memory, orientation, attention, recall, and language" (Introduction, p.1799).
- **Boundary conditions**: The most common instrument used across the 81 reviewed studies (n=38); the Discussion notes MMSE has "greater sensitivity towards the advanced stages of AD" relative to CDR.
- **Related concepts**: CDR-SB, Disease Severity Staging Instrument

## Global Deterioration Scale (GDS)
- **Notation**: —
- **Definition**: Assesses cognitive and functional capability "additionally includ[ing] the behavioural status of the individual, although the latter is often based on subjective results" (Introduction, p.1799).
- **Boundary conditions**: Not specified in paper beyond this definitional statement; usage frequency across the 81 studies not separately reported (grouped in Table S2).
- **Related concepts**: CDR-SB, MMSE

## Dependence Scale (DS)
- **Notation**: —
- **Definition**: "Measures the gradient loss of independence and measures the amount of assistance the patient requires; it is often used in tandem with another instrument to determine dementia severity level" (Introduction, p.1799).
- **Boundary conditions**: Cited as used in combination with CDR in at least one included study (Michaud et al. 2017 [31]).
- **Related concepts**: CDR-SB, FAST

## Direct Costs (Medical and Non-Medical)
- **Notation**: —
- **Definition**: "Expenditures related to medical and non-medical care" (Introduction, p.1799). Direct medical costs = healthcare expenditures (inpatient, outpatient, medications, diagnostics, hospice, etc.); direct non-medical costs = social/community care costs and other non-medical expenditures (daycare, institutional care, financial aid, police call-outs, financial delinquencies) as detailed in Fig. 1.
- **Boundary conditions**: Direct costs comprised 31–52% of total societal costs in mild-stage studies (C09); typically similar or higher in earlier vs. later disease stages for the medical subcomponent, but non-medical direct costs (e.g., long-term care) tend to rise with severity.
- **Related concepts**: Indirect Costs, Societal Cost, Cost Component Taxonomy (Fig. 1)

## Indirect Costs (Informal Caregiving)
- **Notation**: —
- **Definition**: "Caregiver's loss of productivity or leisure time due to time spent taking care of or supervising the patient, and, where reported, patient's loss of productivity" (Methods, "Definitions of Cost Types", p.1801–1802).
- **Boundary conditions**: Calculated using one of four named costing methods (opportunity, replacement, friction, human capital — Table 2); comprised the largest single share of societal cost, exceeding 50% at the moderate stage in granular-breakdown studies (C02).
- **Related concepts**: Costing Method (Opportunity/Replacement/Friction/Human Capital), Direct Costs, Caregiver Burden

## Costing Method (Opportunity / Replacement / Friction / Human Capital)
- **Notation**: —
- **Definition**: Four alternative methods for monetizing informal caregiving time, per Table 2: Opportunity cost assigns "the market value of lost production time for working caregivers and the value of lost leisure time for non-working caregivers"; Replacement cost assigns "the cost of informal care at the level of remuneration that is required to hire a professional caregiver"; Friction cost is the "cost to employers due [to] the time spent to replace ill workers and the corresponding expenses required to train new replacement employees"; Human capital cost "assigns the cost of informal care based on the cost of absenteeism (i.e., loss of earnings and loss of productivity)."
- **Boundary conditions**: Opportunity costing was used most often across the 81 studies (n=28 studies), followed by replacement costing (n=8 studies).
- **Related concepts**: Indirect Costs (Informal Caregiving)

## PPPY / PPPM (Per-Person-Per-Year / Per-Person-Per-Month)
- **Notation**: PPPY, PPPM
- **Definition**: Standardized cost-reporting units used throughout the review's tables; per Methods, "PPPM costs were converted to per person per year (PPPY) costs by multiplying PPPM costs by 12" to enable comparison across studies reporting costs at different time granularities.
- **Boundary conditions**: Applied uniformly to convert reported monthly costs to annual costs; caregiver time units were separately converted to hours per person per week.
- **Related concepts**: Direct Costs, Indirect Costs, Societal Cost

## Societal Cost
- **Notation**: —
- **Definition**: The sum of all direct (medical + non-medical) and indirect (informal caregiving + patient productivity loss) costs attributable to AD/dementia, inclusive of the previously under-reported cost categories this review specifically searched for (financial delinquency, public fiscal loss, intangible QoL loss).
- **Boundary conditions**: "The proportions of individual components making up total cost were individually calculated for each study by dividing each component by the total summed cost (i.e., all indirect, direct components summed) for each severity" (Methods, "Data Extraction").
- **Related concepts**: Direct Costs, Indirect Costs, Cost Component Taxonomy (Fig. 1)

## Intangible Cost (Quality-of-Life Loss)
- **Notation**: —
- **Definition**: "The monetary value associated with losses in quality of life" (Table 5 footnote) — e.g., mental and emotional strain experienced by patients and caregivers due to AD, monetized as a cost category.
- **Boundary conditions**: "Not well characterized in the literature"; only identified with severity-stratified data in one reviewed study (Monfared et al. 2024 [59]), which found opposite severity trends for patients (rising) vs. caregivers (falling) (C10).
- **Related concepts**: Indirect Costs, Caregiver Burden

## Financial Delinquency
- **Notation**: —
- **Definition**: An under-reported societal cost category encompassing scams, missed payments/debts, poor credit scores, and overspending resulting from AD-related decline in personal financial management capability; described in the Introduction as among the first functional changes attributable to dementia.
- **Boundary conditions**: Quantified in this review chiefly via the UK Alzheimer's Society 2024 report [20] (Table 5) and studies by Nicholas et al. 2021 [19] and Gresentz et al. 2024 [57]; one Japanese study found 58.1% of dementia patients with financial problems reported "a large amount of unnecessary shopping" as the most frequent issue.
- **Related concepts**: Cost Component Taxonomy (Fig. 1), Societal Cost

## Care Setting (Community vs. Institutional)
- **Notation**: —
- **Definition**: The location in which a patient with AD/dementia receives care — "community care" (living at home, potentially with domiciliary/day care support) versus "institutional care" (residential/nursing care facilities) — used throughout the review as a stratification axis alongside severity.
- **Boundary conditions**: Only 1 of the 81 included studies was set specifically in institutional care alone (33 studies were community-care-only; the remainder mixed or unspecified); institutional care is direct-cost-dominated (C08) whereas community care is informal-cost-dominated (C02).
- **Related concepts**: Direct Costs, Indirect Costs

## Cost Component Taxonomy (Fig. 1)
- **Notation**: —
- **Definition**: The paper's own structured hierarchy of all societal cost components considered, organized under "Societal cost" → {Direct cost {Direct medical, Direct non-medical}, Indirect cost {Loss of productivity & leisure time (Caregiver), Loss of productivity (Patient), Patient & caregiver: Public fiscal losses / Loss of QoL costs}}.
- **Boundary conditions**: See [logic/solution/cost_taxonomy.md](solution/cost_taxonomy.md) for the fully reconstructed hierarchy with every leaf category from Fig. 1.
- **Related concepts**: Direct Costs, Indirect Costs, Societal Cost, Financial Delinquency, Intangible Cost
