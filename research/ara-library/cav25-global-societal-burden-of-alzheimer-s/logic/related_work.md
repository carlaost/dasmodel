# Related Work

This paper is itself a literature review; its "related work" is the set of prior reviews, cost-of-illness studies, and cohort studies it cites as background, baselines, or raw data sources. Works with a specific technical delta relative to this review get full `RW` blocks; the remaining citations that materially shaped the review (background context, raw-data source studies feeding the review's own tables) are captured more briefly afterward to preserve the paper's full citation footprint.

## RW01: Wimo et al. 2022 — "The worldwide costs of dementia in 2019" [1]
- **DOI**: Not specified in paper (journal: Alzheimers Dement. 2022;19(7):2865–73)
- **Type**: baseline
- **Delta**:
  - What changed: This review narrows Wimo et al.'s global, undifferentiated $1313 billion (2019) total-cost estimate (50% attributed to informal care) down to a severity-, care-setting-, and cost-category-stratified breakdown across 81 individual studies.
  - Why: A single global point estimate cannot inform severity-specific health-economic modeling of disease-modifying treatments; the stratified view is needed to model how costs change as a treatment delays progression through stages.
- **Claims affected**: C01, C02
- **Adopted elements**: The ~50% informal-care share of total cost is consistent with (and reproduced at a more granular level by) this review's C02 finding.

## RW02: Sontheimer, Konnopka & König 2021 — "The excess costs of dementia: a systematic review and meta-analysis" [2]
- **DOI**: Not specified in paper (J Alzheimer's Dis. 2021;83(1):333–54)
- **Type**: baseline
- **Delta**:
  - What changed: Provides an excess-cost (vs. non-demented population) meta-analytic estimate; this review instead stratifies absolute cost by severity stage without a non-demented comparison group.
  - Why: Cited as general background evidence for the cost burden of dementia rather than as a source of severity-stratified data.
- **Claims affected**: none directly (background citation)
- **Adopted elements**: General framing of dementia as cost-driving.

## RW03: Tahami Monfared, Byrnes, White & Zhang 2022 — "The humanistic and economic burden of Alzheimer's disease" [3]
- **DOI**: Not specified in paper (Neurol Ther. 2022;11(2):525–51)
- **Type**: imports
- **Delta**:
  - What changed: A prior narrative review of AD's humanistic and economic burden; this review imports its general framing but adds systematic severity/care-setting stratification plus the under-reported cost categories (financial delinquency, public fiscal loss, intangible QoL costs).
  - Why: Establishes the pre-existing recognition of AD's economic burden that this review seeks to make more granular and comprehensive.
- **Claims affected**: none directly (background citation, repeatedly cited alongside caregiver-burden discussion)
- **Adopted elements**: Framing of AD's dual humanistic/economic burden.

## RW04: Jonsson, Tate, Frisell & Wimo 2022 — "The costs of dementia in Europe: an updated review and meta-analysis" [6]
- **DOI**: Not specified in paper (Pharmacoeconomics. 2022;41(1):59–75)
- **Type**: bounds
- **Delta**:
  - What changed: Jonsson et al.'s review is explicitly named in the Discussion as an example of a prior review "more narrowly scoped by region" (Europe); this review's contribution is explicitly to go beyond single-region scoping.
  - Why: To argue for the added value of a multi-region, severity-stratified synthesis over region-specific reviews.
- **Claims affected**: C04 (regional variability), problem.md G1
- **Adopted elements**: The 55-million 2019 global dementia prevalence figure used in this review's Introduction is sourced from this reference.

## RW05: Tay, Ong, Tay, Ng & Parumasivam 2024 — "Economic burden of Alzheimer's disease: a systematic review" [5]
- **DOI**: Not specified in paper (Value Health Reg Issues. 2024;40:1–12)
- **Type**: baseline
- **Delta**:
  - What changed: The closest prior systematic review in scope; this review's Discussion directly cites Tay et al.'s finding that institutional/formal caregiving increases total direct cost by up to 67.3% of overall AD economic burden, using it to corroborate this review's own care-setting finding (C08) rather than superseding it outright.
  - Why: Used as a corroborating comparator for the institutional-care direct-cost finding, and as a data source (some individual studies underlying Tay et al.'s review, e.g., Wang 2008 and Rapp 2017, appear directly in this review's Table 3/Table 4 with a footnote "Study obtained from Tay et al. 2023 [5]").
- **Claims affected**: C08
- **Adopted elements**: The 67.3% institutional-care direct-cost-increase figure; several underlying primary studies extracted via this review.

## RW06: Deb, Thornton, Sambamoorthi & Innes 2017 — "Direct and indirect cost of managing Alzheimer's disease and related dementias in the United States" [18]
- **DOI**: Not specified in paper (Expert Rev Pharmacoecon Outcomes Res. 2017;17(3):189–202)
- **Type**: bounds
- **Delta**:
  - What changed: A US-specific cost review; this review's Discussion notes it as an example of prior reviews narrowly scoped by region (North America), which this review's multi-region design goes beyond.
  - Why: Motivates the multi-region scope of this review as an improvement over region-limited prior reviews.
- **Claims affected**: problem.md G1
- **Adopted elements**: None directly reused; cited as a scoping contrast.

## RW07: Mattap et al. 2022 — "The economic burden of dementia in low- and middle-income countries (LMICs): a systematic review" [48]
- **DOI**: Not specified in paper (BMJ Glob Health. 2022;7(4):e007409)
- **Type**: bounds
- **Delta**:
  - What changed: A prior review specifically scoped to LMICs; this review's inclusion set skews toward Europe (n=37) and does not systematically emphasize LMIC representation, so Mattap et al. covers a complementary geographic gap this review does not close.
  - Why: Cited in the "regional variability" discussion of cost drivers.
- **Claims affected**: C04
- **Adopted elements**: None directly reused (background/contrast citation).

## RW08: Nicholas, Langa, Bynum & Hsu 2021 — "Financial presentation of Alzheimer disease and related dementias" [19]
- **DOI**: Not specified in paper (JAMA Intern Med. 2021;181(2):220–7)
- **Type**: imports
- **Delta**:
  - What changed: Provides evidence that financial-management decline is an early, quantifiable feature of dementia; this review imports this framing to justify searching specifically for financial-delinquency costs as part of societal burden (a category most AD cost-of-illness studies omit).
  - Why: Establishes the clinical/financial link motivating the review's G2 gap (under-reported non-medical costs) and the C05 claim (financial delinquency costs).
- **Claims affected**: C05
- **Adopted elements**: The framing of financial delinquency as an early dementia-attributable cost.

## RW09: Alzheimer's Society (UK) 2024 — "The economic impact of dementia. Module 1: Annual costs of dementia" [20]
- **DOI**: Not specified in paper (grey literature report)
- **Type**: imports
- **Delta**:
  - What changed: A grey-literature report (not a peer-reviewed cost-of-illness study) that is this review's sole source for the UK-specific unique-burden figures (heating bills, police call-outs, power of attorney, reduced mobility, scams, reduced consumer spending) reported by severity in Table 5.
  - Why: Identified via the review's grey-literature hand-search arm specifically because peer-reviewed AD cost-of-illness studies rarely report these categories.
- **Claims affected**: C05
- **Adopted elements**: All UK unique-burden values in Table 5 are taken directly from this source.

## RW10: Martins, Kotsopoulos, Michalowsky, Pemberton-Ross, Urbich & Connolly 2022 — "Evaluation of the fiscal costs and consequences of Alzheimer's disease in Germany: microsimulation of patients' and caregivers' pathways" [68]
- **DOI**: Not specified in paper (J Prev Alzheimers Dis. 2022;9(4):758–68)
- **Type**: imports
- **Delta**:
  - What changed: A microsimulation study providing this review's only source for public-fiscal-loss figures (lifetime direct tax loss, net incremental government/social-security loss, fiscal public loss/financial support) — a cost category absent from conventional AD cost-of-illness studies.
  - Why: Selected to fill the review's targeted gap around public fiscal impact (G2), separate from patient/caregiver-borne costs.
- **Claims affected**: C06
- **Adopted elements**: All Germany public-fiscal-loss figures in Table 5 and the €74,288 net incremental loss figure.

## RW11: van Dyck et al. 2023 — "Lecanemab in early Alzheimer's disease" [22]; Donanemab reference entry [23]
- **DOI**: Not specified for [22] in this paper (N Engl J Med. 2023;388(1):9–21)
- **Type**: imports (motivating background, not a cost-of-illness data source)
- **Delta**:
  - What changed: These describe the disease-modifying anti-amyloid treatments (lecanemab, donanemab) that motivate the review's stated purpose (O4) — understanding cost-by-severity to evaluate the economic benefit of therapies that keep patients in earlier, less costly stages longer.
  - Why: Cited only in the Introduction as motivation; not used as a source of cost data.
- **Claims affected**: none directly (motivational background for the review's framing, referenced in problem.md O4)
- **Adopted elements**: None (motivational citation only).

## RW12: GERAS observational study series — Wimo et al. 2013 [24], Reed et al. 2016 [26], Dodel et al. 2015 [28], Bruno et al. 2018 [34], Lenox-Smith et al. 2016 [35], Olazaran et al. 2017 [36], Rapp et al. 2018 [51], Nakanishi et al. 2020/2021 [54, 55], Chandler et al. 2023 [49], Jones et al. 2017 [50]
- **DOI**: Not specified in paper (multiple journals; see reference list entries 24, 26, 28, 34–36, 49–51, 54–55)
- **Type**: imports (primary raw-data source studies)
- **Delta**:
  - What changed: These are not reviews but a linked family of prospective observational cohort studies (the GERAS study, run in Europe, the US [GERAS-US], and Japan [GERAS-J]) that supply a large share of this review's own Tables 3, 4, and 6 community-care cost-by-severity values across France, Germany, the UK, the US, and Japan.
  - Why: Selected because they report cost stratified by discrete AD severity — the review's core inclusion requirement — across multiple regions using a harmonized study design.
- **Claims affected**: C01, C04, C09
- **Adopted elements**: A large fraction of the raw community-care cost values underlying Tables 3, 4, and 6.

## RW13: Jetsonen, Kuvaja-Kollner, Valimaki, Selander, Martikainen & Koivisto 2021 — "Total cost of care increases significantly from early to mild Alzheimer's disease: 5-year ALSOVA follow-up" [30]
- **DOI**: Not specified in paper (Age Ageing. 2021;50(6):2116–22)
- **Type**: imports (primary raw-data source study)
- **Delta**:
  - What changed: The single study providing this review's cited 126% MCI-to-mild cost increase (C03) and one of the few sources reporting MCI-stage cost data at all (Tables 3, 4, 6).
  - Why: One of the only longitudinal cohort studies in the corpus reporting cost at the MCI/very-mild stage specifically, filling gap G3.
- **Claims affected**: C03
- **Adopted elements**: All Finland MCI/mild/moderate/severe cost values in Tables 3, 4, and 6.

## RW14: Tahami Monfared, Khachatryan, Hummel, Kopiec, Martinez & Zhang 2024 — "Assessing quality of life, economic burden, and independence across the Alzheimer's disease continuum using patient-caregiver dyad surveys" [59]
- **DOI**: Not specified in paper (J Alzheimers Dis. 2024;99(1):191–206)
- **Type**: imports (primary raw-data source study)
- **Delta**:
  - What changed: The sole source for this review's intangible (QoL-loss) cost-by-severity data for both patients and caregivers (C10), and one of the few US studies reporting a severity-stratified overall-severity cost stratum.
  - Why: Specifically selected to fill the review's targeted gap around intangible/QoL costs (G2), which conventional cost-of-illness studies rarely quantify.
- **Claims affected**: C10
- **Adopted elements**: All Monfared 2024 intangible-cost PPPM values in Table 5.
