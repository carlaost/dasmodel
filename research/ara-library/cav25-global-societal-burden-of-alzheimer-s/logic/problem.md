# Problem Specification

## Observations

### O1: Dementia prevalence is large and rapidly growing
- **Statement**: "The global prevalence of dementia was reported to be 55 million cases in 2019 alone, with cases expected to more than double by 2050 [6]."
- **Evidence**: Introduction, p.1798, citing Jonsson et al. 2022 [6].
- **Implication**: The population base subject to AD/dementia-related societal cost is large and growing, making cost characterization a growing policy priority.

### O2: Existing point estimates of AD/dementia cost are large but fragmented by cost type
- **Statement**: "in the United States (US) alone, direct medical and non-medical costs were $154 billion USD (2015)... Globally, the total cost of dementia has been estimated to be $1313 billion as of 2019, with 50% of this cost attributed to informal care (e.g., unpaid caregivers) [1]."
- **Evidence**: Introduction, p.1799, citing references [1] and [4].
- **Implication**: Existing global/national estimates already show informal care as roughly half of total cost, but do not stratify systematically by severity, care setting, or region within a single unified review.

### O3: Day-to-day functional/behavioral impacts of AD carry costs that are rarely quantified
- **Statement**: "diminished mental acuity and changed behavior owing to cognitive decline can lead to additional societal costs resulting from impairment observed in day-to-day life (e.g., impaired personal financial managements leading to financial delinquency and police call-outs...). While anecdotes... are prevalent, studies quantifying this type of impairment in costs are limited [19–21]."
- **Evidence**: Introduction, p.1799–1800.
- **Implication**: A cost review restricted to conventional direct/indirect cost buckets would miss financial delinquency, police call-outs, and similar burdens that are specific to a cognitive-decline disease.

### O4: Disease-modifying treatments raise the stakes of understanding cost by severity
- **Statement**: "Anti-amyloid monoclonal antibodies, a pharmacological treatment designed to delay disease progression of AD, have recently been introduced to the treatment landscape [22] [23]. Such treatments may have a profound impact on reducing the growing economic burden... To better evaluate the health economic benefits of such therapies, a detailed understanding of both direct and indirect costs is essential."
- **Evidence**: Introduction, p.1800, citing lecanemab [22] and donanemab [23].
- **Implication**: Cost data stratified by severity stage is a prerequisite input for health-economic modeling of disease-modifying treatments that aim to keep patients in earlier (less costly) stages longer.

### O5: 81 studies span more than a decade and multiple regions but use inconsistent severity definitions
- **Statement**: The final selection included 81 studies (59 prospective/retrospective studies, 2 AD reports, 20 SLRs/meta-analyses); "The Mini-Mental State Examination (MMSE) was the most common disease severity classification system used (n = 38), followed by the clinical dementia rating (CDR) system (n = 6)."
- **Evidence**: Results, "Study Selection and Characteristics", p.1804–1805; Fig. 2; Fig. 3.
- **Implication**: Any cross-study synthesis of cost-by-severity must reconcile at least five different staging instruments (MMSE, CDR-SB, FAST, GDS, DS) that are not directly interchangeable.

## Gaps

### G1: No prior review holistically characterizes AD/dementia societal cost across severity, care setting, cost type, and region simultaneously
- **Statement**: "Despite the depth of evidence, reviews lack a holistic assessment of such costs, falling short of illustrating unmet medical needs."
- **Caused by**: O1, O2.
- **Existing attempts**: Prior systematic reviews narrower in scope by region (Europe [6, 69], North America [18, 86], Asia [87]) or lacking stratification by severity/setting, per the Discussion's "Strengths, Limitations, and Future Direction" section.
- **Why they fail**: Regional or setting-specific scoping precludes a single comparative framework across severity stages, care settings, and cost categories at once.

### G2: Under-reported, non-medical societal costs are rarely captured in AD-specific cost-of-illness studies
- **Statement**: "including societal burdens not often reported by AD-specific cost-of-illness studies such as financial delinquencies" (Abstract); costs of scams, police call-outs, heating bills, power of attorney, and reduced consumer spending are addressed only by a handful of studies (e.g., the UK Alzheimer's Society Report [20], Martins et al. 2022 [68]).
- **Caused by**: O3.
- **Existing attempts**: Individual studies (e.g., UK Alzheimer's Society 2024 [20]; Nicholas et al. 2021 [19]; Gresentz et al. 2024 [57]) report isolated pieces of this burden.
- **Why they fail**: These costs are scattered across disparate, non-AD-specific literatures (financial regulation, criminology-adjacent, public finance) rather than consolidated cost-of-illness studies, so they are typically omitted from standard AD economic-burden reviews.

### G3: MCI-stage cost evidence is sparse relative to diagnosed-AD stages
- **Statement**: "While studies reporting costs of mild cognitive impairment (MCI) were limited, the economic burden reported for this stage was appreciable compared to mild AD."
- **Caused by**: O5 (few of the 81 studies report an MCI-specific cost stratum; e.g., Table 3 shows MCI/very-mild costs reported by only about a third of the community-care studies listed).
- **Existing attempts**: A minority of studies (e.g., Jetsonen et al. 2021 [30], Dauphinot et al. 2022 [52], Monfared et al. 2023/2024 [45, 59]) do report MCI-specific costs.
- **Why they fail**: Most cost-of-illness studies enroll patients only once a formal AD diagnosis (mild/moderate/severe) has been made, so pre-diagnosis or MCI-stage cost is systematically under-sampled.

## Key Insight
- **Insight**: A targeted (not systematic) literature review that (a) harmonizes five heterogeneous severity-staging instruments into four common buckets (MCI, mild, moderate, severe), (b) extracts costs by both care setting and cost type without inflating/converting to a common cost-year or currency, and (c) deliberately searches for previously under-reported cost categories (financial delinquency, public fiscal loss, intangible QoL loss) can produce a single comparative framework spanning cost category, severity, care setting, and region that no prior review achieves.
- **Derived from**: O1–O5, G1–G3.
- **Enables**: A unified conceptual cost-definition framework (Fig. 1's cost-component taxonomy) that can be reused as an input specification for future regional health-economic evaluations of disease-modifying AD therapies.

## Assumptions
- A1: Disease severity across the 81 studies' differing staging scales (MMSE, CDR-SB, FAST, GDS, DS) can be validly harmonized into four buckets — MCI/very mild, mild (including mild-to-moderate), moderate, and severe (including moderately severe/severe, very severe) — "regardless of the dementia staging scales used by different studies" (Methods, "Data Extraction").
- A2: Reported costs are left in their original cost year and currency ("with no modification of cost year or currency"), trading cross-study comparability for fidelity to the source data.
- A3: Where a study reports a cost as a range within a severity stage, the median value is used as the single representative estimate (Methods, "Data Extraction", citing ref. [30]).
- A4: Per-person-per-month (PPPM) costs are converted to per-person-per-year (PPPY) by multiplying by 12, and caregiver time units are converted to hours per person per week, to standardize comparison across studies.
- A5: A single reviewer performed study selection/data extraction with second-reviewer quality-assurance checking (not independent dual review of every record), and disagreements were adjudicated by a third reviewer.
