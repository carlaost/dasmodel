abstract: "Alzheimer disease (AD) is the most common cause of dementia and represents a major health and social care challenge in Spain, with an estimated prevalence of 700,000-800,000 affected individuals. The MapEA project (Map of Alzheimer's disease and other dementias in Spain), launched in 2017, revealed territorial heterogeneity, diagnostic limitations, and deficits in care coordination in AD management. Consequently, an updated assessment was conducted in 2024 to identify progress, areas for improvement, and recommendations to optimize comprehensive care. A four-phase study was carried out: a literature review, the development and validation of a digital questionnaire distributed to 138 professionals from different fields, analysis by autonomous communities, and integration of the information through descriptive and thematic analyses with a multidisciplinary Advisory Committee. Results showed a low-to-moderate level of implementation and awareness of national strategies, with only four autonomous communities having specific regional plans. Compared with 2017, a reduction in care resources was identified, although a significant increase was observed in diagnostic technologies and in specialized training positions. Surveys reflected substantial clinical research activity; however, delays in diagnostic testing persisted, along with unequal availability of non-pharmacological therapies often dependent on private funding or co-payment. # Proyecto MapEA: evolución de la asistencia a la EA y otras demencias en España (2017-2024)

## Overview

This is a Spanish-language observational **mapping / survey study** (article type: *Revisión*) that updates the 2017 MapEA report to track how care for Alzheimer disease (AD) and other dementias evolved in Spain between 2017 and 2024. It is not an interventional trial and collected **no patient-level data** — the evidence base is a validated digital questionnaire answered by 138 professionals across seven profiles (Neurology, Geriatrics, Psychiatry, Primary Care, Social Work, Health Management, and family associations), plus a documentary review and integration with a seven-member multidisciplinary Advisory Committee.

The study is organized around four thematic axes: (1) planning and organization tools (national strategies and regional plans), (2) specific resources for comprehensive care (care units, imaging technology, training positions, research, associative network, dependency-care resources), (3) the care pathway (subprocesses S0–S6, from first symptoms to palliative care), and (4) transversal challenges and recommendations. Its central finding is a mixed picture: real progress in diagnostic technology capacity, specialized training, and research activity, set against persistent territorial inequalities, coordination deficits between care levels, human-resource shortages, and inequity of access (especially for non-pharmacological therapies and advanced diagnostics).

The paper is compiled here **full-text** from a CC-BY open-access PDF (10 pages). The three supplementary tables (Tabla S1, S2, S3) referenced in the text are online-only (Anexo — Material adicional) and are **not** contained in the provided PDF; they are recorded as unavailable in the evidence index.
