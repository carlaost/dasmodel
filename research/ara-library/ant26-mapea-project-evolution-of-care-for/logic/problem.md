# Problem Specification

All numbers are copied exactly from the source PDF. Percentages are survey-response
proportions among the 138 surveyed professionals ("expertos encuestados") unless noted.
Page references (p.N) are to `paper.pdf`.

## Observations

### O1: AD is the leading dementia cause and a large, growing burden in Spain
- **Statement**: AD is the most common cause of dementia worldwide (60-80% of cases); in Spain an estimated 700,000-800,000 people are affected (abstract). Recent reports estimated >909,000 people with dementia in Spain in 2022 (~2.2% of the total population) and >850,000 people with mild cognitive impairment attributable to AD currently.
- **Evidence**: Abstract; Introduction, p.2 («más de 909.000 personas con demencia … el 2,2% de la población … más de 850.000 personas presentan deterioro cognitivo leve atribuible a EA»).
- **Implication**: A high sociosanitary burden makes AD a public-health priority and justifies periodic national mapping.

### O2: The 2017 MapEA report exposed heterogeneity and gaps
- **Statement**: The first MapEA study (2017/2018) found great heterogeneity between autonomous communities (CC.AA.), scarce implementation of specific plans, limited/poorly-known care resources, early-detection shortfalls, inequity in access to diagnostic tests, and limited coordination between care levels.
- **Evidence**: Introduction, p.2 (summary of the 2017 report, ref 6).
- **Implication**: A 2024 re-assessment was needed to measure change and update recommendations.

### O3: Diagnostic imaging capacity grew markedly 2017→2024
- **Statement**: CT (TAC) units 754→924 (+23%); MRI (RM) 559→803 (+44%); SPECT 91→185 (+103%); PET 66→135 (+105%).
- **Evidence**: Results, p.3 (verbatim increments); Table/figures none — these are national equipment counts.
- **Implication**: Technological capacity for early diagnosis improved substantially.

### O4: Specialized training offer expanded 2017→2024
- **Statement**: 2024 MIR positions: Neurology 190, Psychiatry 332, Geriatrics 126, Family & Community Medicine (MFyC) 2,526 — increases vs 2017 of +55% (123), +38% (241), +94% (65), +40% (1,810) respectively.
- **Evidence**: Results, p.3.
- **Implication**: The professional pipeline for AD-relevant specialties widened.

### O5: Care units slightly contracted; research and associative network grew
- **Statement**: 108 hospitals with monographic consultations / specialized dementia units (vs 116 in 2017); 30 EAIA-TC in Catalonia (vs 29 in 2017); 33 accredited research institutes with active dementia/AD lines; 139 AD clinical trials ongoing per the Spanish Clinical Trials Registry at study close; 335 AFA associations (+10% vs 305 in 2017).
- **Evidence**: Results, p.3–4 (Fig. 2A consultations; Fig. 2B clinical trials).
- **Implication**: Diagnostic-unit provision did not keep pace with technology/training growth, whereas research and family-association capacity increased.

### O6: Diagnostic-test access and waiting times remain unequal
- **Statement**: Reported access: CT 90%, MRI 82%; use in suspected AD: CT 75%, MRI 54%. Waiting >3 months: >30% (CT) / >50% (MRI); deemed inadequate by 52% (CT) and 61% (MRI). CSF (LCR) biomarkers (T-tau, P-tau, Ab42) available in 77% of centers, done in 35% of suspected AD; ApoE access 60% (done 17%), familial-forms test access 64% (done 18%). Nuclear-medicine tests: access 57-83%, use 19-28%.
- **Evidence**: Results, p.5–6 (Fig. 4; Tabla S3, supplementary/not in PDF).
- **Implication**: Availability does not translate into timely, equitable use; advanced diagnostics remain under-used.

### O7: Care pathway shows persistent coordination deficits
- **Statement**: Derivation protocol reported by 65% of experts; referral to Neurology after 2-5 primary-care visits per 83%; waiting to hospital care 3-6 months (33%) or >6 months (33%). 82% reported no known integral follow-up protocol. Only 49% (follow-up), 52% (palliative), 59% (pharmacological) and 70% (non-pharmacological) said the described model fits usual practice.
- **Evidence**: Results, p.4–7 (Fig. 3 care pathway).
- **Implication**: Structural coordination between primary and hospital care and with social services is a recurrent bottleneck, especially diagnostic delay.

## Gaps

### G1: No centralized national registry of AD care resources
- **Statement**: The absence of a centralized national registry hinders a precise view of care-unit characteristics, degree of implementation, and time evolution at national level.
- **Caused by**: O5.
- **Existing attempts**: MapEA 2017 counted resources ad hoc; 2024 repeats via expert reports + public sources.
- **Why they fail**: Without a standing registry, comparisons across years are confounded by changing information sources and record completeness (see constraints).

### G2: Territorial inequality and weak regional planning
- **Statement**: Only four CC.AA. (Andalucía 2023, Cataluña 2022, Extremadura 2007, Comunidad Valenciana 2006) have AD-specific regional plans; half of these strategies predate 2019 and have not been revised. Implementation/awareness of the national Strategy and Plan is low-to-moderate in most CC.AA.
- **Caused by**: O2, O7.
- **Existing attempts**: National Strategy (2016), Plan Integral 2019-2023, "Abordaje del Alzheimer y otras demencias" (2022).
- **Why they fail**: Low implementation and uneven regional uptake sustain heterogeneity and inequity.

### G3: Inequitable access to advanced diagnostics and non-pharmacological therapies
- **Statement**: Advanced biomarker/genetic testing is available but under-used, and non-pharmacological therapies frequently depend on private funding or co-payment, evidencing a structural lack of public financing.
- **Caused by**: O6, and Table 1 usage/availability gaps.
- **Existing attempts**: Existing service portfolios and private/association provision.
- **Why they fail**: No structural public financing → inequity of access, especially rural vs urban.

### G4: Human-resource shortage and fragmented, non-person-centered care
- **Statement**: Despite more training positions, limited availability of Neurology/Geriatrics/Psychiatry specialists, workload overload, and poor inter-level coordination persist; comprehensive, person-centered care remains an unmet objective.
- **Caused by**: O4, O7.
- **Existing attempts**: Shared care models across levels.
- **Why they fail**: Specialist scarcity + coordination gaps + no standardized protocols integrated in health-information systems.

## Key Insight
- **Insight**: Measuring AD-care evolution requires decomposing "care" into distinct, comparable dimensions (planning tools, physical/human resources, and a subprocess-level care pathway) surveyed from front-line professionals across all CC.AA. — so that genuine progress (technology, training, research) can be separated from persistent, structurally-financed inequities (coordination, human resources, equity of access).
- **Derived from**: O2, O3–O7.
- **Enables**: A four-phase mixed-methods design plus a care-pathway model (S0–S6) with an added transversal support subprocess (S0), producing actionable per-dimension recommendations.

## Assumptions
- A1: Expert survey responses (n=138) are a valid proxy for real-world availability, use, and waiting times when patient-level data are not collected.
- A2: The modal (most-frequent) response per CC.AA. adequately summarizes strategy implementation/awareness (basis of Fig. 1).
- A3: Similar analysis criteria between 2017 and 2024 make temporal comparison meaningful (the paper flags this must be interpreted with caution — see constraints).
- A4: Public-source counts (equipment, training positions, trials, associations) are reasonably complete despite the lack of a centralized registry.
