# Papers Block Scientific Progress

Scientific papers have become the archnemesis of efficient scientific progress. While individual papers may merit reading, they represent a fundamental bottleneck preventing faster discovery and transformation.

The paper has become synonymous with scientific output itself. Researchers work "on a paper" rather than pursuing ideas or discoveries, and they "publish a paper" rather than completing research. This creates a measurement problem: what metrics actually indicate work quality?

Citation counts emerge as the primary measurable proxy. They indicate whether the scientific community finds a paper relevant enough to reference in their own work. However, this conflates "influence with quality, reference with endorsement."

Once papers become the canonical unit of scientific output, citations become the scalable comparison method. This transforms citations from a relevance indicator into a proxy for scientific merit itself. The system then incentivizes scientists to optimize for citation generation rather than discovery.

According to Goodhart's Law, "when a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure." When careers, grants, and prestige depend on citations, scientists naturally gravitate toward highly citable work: review papers, positive results, broad confident narratives. Unrewarded activities — negative results, replications, challenging established ideas — get neglected.

The proposed solution typically involves developing new metrics, yet most innovations still measure papers themselves. The paper remains unquestioned as the fundamental unit of scientific output.

The author argues that lasting change requires reconceptualizing what science actually is, beyond the paper format itself.
